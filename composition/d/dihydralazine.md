# Dihydralazine

**Generic Name: Dihydralazine**

**Usage:**

Dihydralazine is primarily used to treat high blood pressure (hypertension), particularly in situations where other medications haven't been effective or are poorly tolerated.  It's a vasodilator, meaning it widens blood vessels, allowing blood to flow more easily, thus reducing the pressure on artery walls.  While its primary use is for hypertension, it might be considered in some cases of severe heart failure, though this is less common due to the availability of other, often safer, treatments.  The use of dihydralazine in heart failure is generally reserved for specific situations and under careful medical supervision.  It's important to note that dihydralazine is not a first-line treatment for hypertension; doctors usually prescribe other medications before resorting to it.


**Dosage:**

Dosage of dihydralazine varies significantly depending on the patient's condition, age, and response to treatment.  A doctor will carefully determine the appropriate dose.  It's crucial to follow the prescribed dosage regimen precisely.  Generally, dihydralazine is available in tablet form.  Dosage for adults typically starts low and is gradually increased as needed to achieve the desired blood pressure control.  There is no established pediatric dosage, and use in children is generally not recommended unless specifically directed by a specialist in exceptional circumstances.  The medication should *never* be administered without a doctor's explicit prescription and guidance.   Intravenous formulations exist but are generally administered in hospital settings.


**Side Effects:**

Common side effects include:

* Headache
* Dizziness
* Nausea
* Vomiting
* Flushing (reddening of the skin)
* Palpitations (rapid or irregular heartbeat)
* Swelling in the ankles or feet (edema)


Less common but more serious side effects include:

* Lupus-like syndrome (a condition mimicking the autoimmune disease lupus) – characterized by joint pain, fever, and rash.  This is a relatively rare but significant risk.
* Low blood pressure (hypotension)
* Angina (chest pain)


If you experience any of these side effects, particularly the more serious ones, consult your doctor immediately.


**How it Works:**

Dihydralazine works by relaxing and widening the blood vessels, primarily the arterioles (the smaller arteries). This vasodilation decreases peripheral vascular resistance (the resistance to blood flow in the blood vessels), leading to a reduction in blood pressure.  It achieves this effect by affecting specific receptors in the smooth muscle cells of blood vessel walls, leading to their relaxation and dilation.

**Precautions:**

* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  Dihydralazine should be avoided during pregnancy unless the benefits clearly outweigh the potential risks.  Consult a doctor immediately if you become pregnant while taking this medication. Use during breastfeeding is generally discouraged due to potential transfer to the infant.
* **Liver and Kidney Disease:**  Individuals with impaired liver or kidney function may require dosage adjustments or close monitoring.
* **Coronary Artery Disease:**  Dihydralazine should be used with caution in patients with coronary artery disease, as it can potentially worsen angina.
* **Drug Interactions:**  Dihydralazine can interact with several other medications, including those for high blood pressure, heart conditions, and depression. Always inform your doctor of all medications you are taking, including over-the-counter drugs and herbal supplements.
* **Older Adults:**  Older adults may be more susceptible to the side effects of dihydralazine and require careful monitoring.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does it take for dihydralazine to work?** A:  The onset of action varies, but a noticeable effect on blood pressure might be observed within a few hours of taking a dose.  However, optimal blood pressure control usually requires several days or weeks of consistent use.
* **Q: Can I stop taking dihydralazine suddenly?** A:  No.  Stopping dihydralazine abruptly can lead to a sudden increase in blood pressure (rebound hypertension), which is dangerous.  Always consult your doctor before stopping the medication, as they will advise on a gradual tapering-off schedule to minimize risks.
* **Q: How should I store dihydralazine?** A:  Store the medication in a cool, dry place, away from direct sunlight and moisture.  Keep it out of the reach of children.
* **Q:  What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Take the missed dose as soon as you remember, unless it's almost time for your next dose.  Do not double the dose to make up for a missed one.
