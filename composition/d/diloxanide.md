# Diloxanide

**Generic Name: Diloxanide**

**Usage:**

Diloxanide is an antiprotozoal medication primarily used to treat intestinal amebiasis, a parasitic infection caused by *Entamoeba histolytica*.  This infection can range from asymptomatic to severe, causing symptoms like diarrhea (sometimes bloody), abdominal cramps, and fever. Diloxanide targets the *Entamoeba histolytica* parasite, eliminating it from the intestines.  It's crucial to understand that diloxanide primarily treats intestinal amebiasis and is *not* effective against extraintestinal amebiasis (where the parasite has spread beyond the intestines).  It's often used in conjunction with other medications, especially in cases of severe infection or when liver involvement is suspected.  Secondary uses are less common and usually involve specific situations guided by a physician's assessment.

**Dosage:**

Dosage varies depending on age and the severity of the infection, always following a doctor's prescription.  Typically, diloxanide furoate (the most common form) is administered orally.  

* **Adults:**  The usual dose is 500 mg three times daily for 10 days.
* **Children:** Dosage for children is typically calculated based on weight, and should always be determined by a physician.  

The medication is usually available in tablet form.  There is no injectable form of diloxanide. It's important to complete the full course of treatment, even if symptoms improve before the end of the prescribed period, to ensure the parasite is fully eradicated.


**Side Effects:**

Common side effects are generally mild and may include:

* Nausea
* Vomiting
* Abdominal cramps
* Diarrhea
* Headache
* Dizziness


Less common, but more serious side effects, though rare, require immediate medical attention. These can include:

* Severe allergic reactions (rash, swelling, difficulty breathing)
* Liver damage (jaundice, dark urine)

If you experience any adverse effects, consult a healthcare professional immediately.


**How it Works:**

Diloxanide furoate works by interfering with the metabolic processes of *Entamoeba histolytica*.  The exact mechanism isn't fully understood, but it's believed to inhibit the parasite's ability to produce essential proteins, ultimately leading to its death and elimination from the body.  This action is specific to the parasite, minimizing harm to the host's cells.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:**  Diloxanide should be avoided in individuals with known hypersensitivity (allergy) to the medication.
* **Drug Interactions:**  While interactions are not commonly reported, it's crucial to inform your doctor about all medications, supplements, and herbal remedies you are taking to minimize potential risks.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  The safety of diloxanide during pregnancy and breastfeeding hasn't been fully established.  Discuss the risks and benefits with your doctor before using this medication during pregnancy or while breastfeeding.
* **Liver Disease:**  Individuals with pre-existing liver conditions should use diloxanide with caution under strict medical supervision, as the drug is processed by the liver.
* **Elderly:**  Older adults may be more susceptible to side effects.  Dosage adjustments may be necessary.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: Can I stop taking diloxanide if my symptoms improve?** A: No. Complete the full course of treatment as prescribed to ensure the infection is fully eradicated.
* **Q: How should I store diloxanide?** A: Store at room temperature, away from moisture and direct sunlight. Keep out of reach of children.
* **Q: Is diloxanide safe for children?** A:  Yes, but only under the supervision of a physician who will determine the appropriate dosage based on the child's weight and health.
* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Take the missed dose as soon as you remember, unless it's almost time for the next dose. Do not double the dose.
* **Q:  Is diloxanide effective against all types of amoebiasis?** A: No. Diloxanide is primarily effective against intestinal amebiasis.  It does not treat amebic liver abscess or other extraintestinal forms of the disease.
