# Daratumumab

**Generic Name: Daratumumab**

**Usage:**

Daratumumab is a type of monoclonal antibody medication primarily used in the treatment of multiple myeloma, a cancer affecting plasma cells in the bone marrow.  Its main role is to target and destroy cancerous plasma cells.  It's often used in combination with other therapies to improve treatment effectiveness.  Beyond multiple myeloma, Daratumumab also plays a significant role in treating certain types of lymphoma, specifically those that are difficult to treat with other methods. These uses are typically in advanced stages of the disease and often involve combinations with other chemotherapy agents.  The precise application depends on the specific type and stage of the cancer, as determined by a healthcare professional.

**Dosage:**

Daratumumab is administered intravenously (IV) – meaning it's given through a drip into a vein.  Dosage and frequency vary considerably depending on the specific disease being treated, the patient's overall health, and other medications being taken.  There is no standard pediatric dosage as its use in children is limited.  A healthcare professional will carefully determine the appropriate dose and schedule for each individual patient.  The medication is not available in tablet or syrup form.

**Side Effects:**

Common side effects can include:

* Infusion-related reactions (fever, chills, nausea, rash)
* Fatigue
* Low blood cell counts (neutropenia, thrombocytopenia, anemia)
* Constipation
* Diarrhea
* Upper respiratory tract infections
* Back pain

Less common but serious side effects can include:

* Severe allergic reactions
* Heart problems
* Kidney problems
* Infections (including serious ones)
* Peripheral neuropathy (nerve damage in the extremities)

**It is crucial to report any side effects, especially if they are severe or persistent, to a doctor immediately.**

**How it Works:**

Daratumumab is a monoclonal antibody.  This means it's a laboratory-made protein designed to target a specific molecule found on the surface of multiple myeloma cells (and some lymphoma cells) – a protein called CD38. By binding to CD38, daratumumab triggers several mechanisms that lead to the destruction of the cancerous cells. These mechanisms include: directly killing the cancer cells, recruiting the body's immune system to attack them, and triggering a process called antibody-dependent cell-mediated cytotoxicity (ADCC). This process essentially marks the cancer cells for destruction by other components of the immune system.

**Precautions:**

* **Allergic Reactions:**  Individuals with known allergies to Daratumumab or its components should avoid using it.
* **Kidney Function:**  Daratumumab can affect kidney function.  Regular monitoring of kidney function is essential, especially in patients with pre-existing kidney disease.
* **Infections:**  Daratumumab can weaken the immune system, increasing the risk of infections. Patients should be vigilant about signs of infection.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  Use during pregnancy and breastfeeding should be carefully considered and only undertaken under strict medical supervision due to potential risks to the developing fetus or infant.
* **Other Medications:**  Daratumumab may interact with other medications. It is vital to inform your doctor about all medications, supplements, and herbal remedies you are taking.
* **Older Adults:**  Older adults may be more susceptible to side effects. Careful monitoring is necessary.
