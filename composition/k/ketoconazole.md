# Ketoconazole

**Generic Name: Ketoconazole**

**Usage:**

Ketoconazole is an antifungal medication primarily used to treat a variety of fungal infections.  Its main application lies in tackling superficial fungal infections like athlete's foot (tinea pedis), jock itch (tinea cruris), ringworm (tinea corporis), and yeast infections (candidiasis) of the skin, mouth (oral thrush), and vagina (vulvovaginal candidiasis).  It's also effective against certain systemic fungal infections, meaning those that affect internal organs, though other antifungals are often preferred for these more serious conditions.  Systemic use is typically reserved for cases like severe fungal infections that don't respond to other treatments or those affecting specific organs, such as coccidioidomycosis or histoplasmosis.  However,  it's important to remember that the use of ketoconazole for systemic infections is declining due to the availability of safer and more effective alternatives. A doctor should always guide the treatment of systemic fungal infections.


**Dosage:**

Dosage varies significantly depending on the type of infection, its severity, the patient's age and weight, and the specific formulation of ketoconazole.  Always follow your doctor's prescribed dosage instructions precisely.  

* **Topical Cream/Ointment/Solution:** Application is usually once or twice daily to the affected area, typically for 2-4 weeks.  Specific instructions will be given by your doctor or pharmacist.

* **Oral Tablets:** Dosage for adults ranges widely depending on the infection being treated, often starting at 200mg once daily and adjusted as needed by the physician.  Children's dosages are carefully calculated based on weight and the specific infection. Oral ketoconazole is not generally recommended for children.

* **Oral Syrup:** This form is less common for ketoconazole but may be prescribed in specific cases for children under the guidance of a physician, following carefully calculated dosages.

**Never adjust the dosage yourself.** Always consult a healthcare professional for any changes to your medication regimen.


**Side Effects:**

* **Common:** Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, abdominal pain, headache, dizziness.

* **Less Common but Serious:** Liver damage (hepatotoxicity),  adrenal insufficiency,  gynecomastia (breast enlargement in men),  changes in blood counts.

* **Rare but Potentially Life-Threatening:** Stevens-Johnson syndrome (a severe skin reaction).

**If you experience any concerning side effects, stop taking the medication and seek immediate medical attention.**


**How it Works:**

Ketoconazole works by inhibiting the synthesis of ergosterol, a vital component of fungal cell membranes.  By disrupting ergosterol production, ketoconazole weakens the fungal cell membrane, leading to cell death and ultimately suppressing the fungal infection.  The drug is selectively toxic to fungi because it targets ergosterol, which is absent in human cells.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Ketoconazole is contraindicated in individuals with known hypersensitivity (allergy) to the drug or its components.  It's also generally avoided in individuals with pre-existing liver disease.

* **Drug Interactions:** Ketoconazole can interact with many other medications, including those metabolized by the liver. It's crucial to inform your doctor about all other medications, herbal supplements, or vitamins you are taking.

* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:** Ketoconazole should be used during pregnancy only if the benefits clearly outweigh the potential risks to the fetus.  Its use during breastfeeding should be carefully considered, and a healthcare professional should be consulted.

* **Children and Older Adults:**  Ketoconazole usage in children and older adults requires careful monitoring due to potential increased risk of side effects. Dosages should be determined by a healthcare provider considering age, weight, and underlying conditions.

* **Alcohol:** Consumption of alcohol should be avoided or minimized while taking ketoconazole due to potential liver damage.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does it take for ketoconazole to work?** A:  This depends on the type and severity of the infection, as well as the formulation used.  Improvement is usually seen within a few days to a few weeks, but a complete course of treatment is generally necessary.

* **Q: Can I stop taking ketoconazole if I feel better?** A: No, completing the full prescribed course is crucial to prevent the infection from recurring.  Stopping early can lead to treatment failure and the development of drug-resistant fungi.

* **Q: How should I store ketoconazole?** A: Store ketoconazole as directed on the product label, typically at room temperature, away from moisture and direct sunlight.

* **Q: Is ketoconazole available over-the-counter?** A: Topical ketoconazole preparations are often available over-the-counter for mild skin infections; however, oral ketoconazole always requires a prescription.

* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: If you miss a dose, take it as soon as you remember, unless it's almost time for your next dose.  Never double up on doses.
