# Sorbitol

**Generic Name: Sorbitol**

**Usage:**

Sorbitol is a sugar alcohol, also known as a polyol.  It's primarily used as a sweetener and humectant (substance that retains moisture) in various food products and pharmaceuticals.  Medically, its main use is as a laxative to relieve constipation.  It works by drawing water into the intestines, softening the stool and making it easier to pass. This makes it particularly helpful for occasional constipation or preparing the bowel for medical procedures like colonoscopies.  Sorbitol may also be found in some oral medications to improve their palatability or enhance their formulation, but this is not its primary medical use.  


**Dosage:**

Dosage of sorbitol varies significantly depending on its intended use and the patient's age and overall health.  Always follow the instructions provided by a healthcare professional or the product labeling.  There is no universally standardized dosage for medical use, as the appropriate amount depends greatly on the severity of constipation. For laxative purposes, oral doses are typically determined on a case-by-case basis. Children's doses will be considerably lower than those for adults.  Different formulations exist:  oral solutions (syrups or liquids) are common for treating constipation, while other formulations might exist for specific applications.  Never exceed the recommended dosage without consulting a doctor.

**Side Effects:**

* **Common:** Gas, bloating, abdominal cramps, diarrhea.  These are often dose-related and may lessen as your body adjusts.
* **Less Common but Serious:** Severe diarrhea, dehydration (especially in children and the elderly).  Dehydration can lead to serious health complications, requiring immediate medical attention.  Allergic reactions (rash, itching, swelling) are also possible, though rare.

If you experience any adverse effects, especially severe diarrhea or dehydration, contact a healthcare professional immediately.

**How it Works:**

Sorbitol is poorly absorbed in the small intestine.  This means that a significant portion of ingested sorbitol reaches the large intestine. Here, it draws water into the intestines via osmosis, increasing the bulk and softness of the stool and stimulating bowel movements.  This laxative effect is primarily osmotic, meaning it works by altering fluid balance in the gut.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Sorbitol should generally be avoided by individuals with known fructose intolerance or malabsorption issues.  It's also not recommended for those with severe gastrointestinal diseases.
* **Drug Interactions:** While not typically associated with significant drug interactions, informing your doctor or pharmacist about all medications you take is always crucial, especially if you have multiple health conditions or are taking other medications affecting the bowels.
* **Vulnerable Groups:**  Children and elderly individuals are at higher risk of dehydration from sorbitol use due to their reduced fluid reserves. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should consult their doctor before using sorbitol, especially in higher dosages.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: Can I use sorbitol every day?**  A: No.  Regular use of sorbitol as a laxative is generally discouraged, as it can lead to dependency and electrolyte imbalances. It's intended for occasional relief of constipation.
* **Q: How should I store sorbitol?** A: Store sorbitol products according to the instructions on the label, usually in a cool, dry place.
* **Q: Is sorbitol safe for everyone?**  A: While generally considered safe when used as directed, sorbitol is not appropriate for everyone. Individuals with certain health conditions should consult their healthcare provider before using it.
* **Q: What should I do if I have severe diarrhea after taking sorbitol?** A:  Severe diarrhea is a sign of potential dehydration and requires immediate medical attention. Contact a doctor or go to the nearest emergency room.
