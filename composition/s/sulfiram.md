# Sulfiram

**Generic Name: Disulfiram** (Note:  Sulfiram is not a commonly used or approved drug name.  The intended drug appears to be Disulfiram.  The information below refers to Disulfiram.)


**Usage:**

Disulfiram is primarily used in the treatment of alcohol dependence.  Its main purpose is to deter individuals from drinking alcohol by causing a very unpleasant reaction if alcohol is consumed while taking the medication. This aversive reaction serves as a strong disincentive to drinking. While not a cure for alcoholism, it can be a valuable tool in conjunction with counseling and other support strategies as part of a comprehensive treatment plan.  It is important to note that disulfiram is only effective if the individual is committed to abstaining from alcohol.  It has no other significant medical uses outside of this context.


**Dosage:**

Dosage of disulfiram is determined by a physician and is highly individualized. Typically, treatment begins with a lower dose (e.g., 250mg daily) and may be gradually increased as tolerated, up to a maximum daily dose of 500mg.  The medication is usually taken once daily, orally, as a tablet.  There are no pediatric formulations, and its use in children is not recommended.  Disulfiram is not typically adjusted based on weight or severity of alcohol dependence, but a doctor will monitor the patient closely to determine the appropriate dose.  The medication should be started only *after* a period of abstinence from alcohol, usually several days, to avoid an adverse reaction.


**Side Effects:**

Common side effects can include:

*   Metallic taste in the mouth
*   Nausea and vomiting
*   Headache
*   Fatigue
*   Drowsiness
*   Rash

Less common, but serious side effects may include:

*   Hepatitis (liver inflammation)
*   Peripheral neuropathy (nerve damage in the extremities)
*   Psychosis
*   Severe allergic reactions

**If you experience any adverse effects, especially those listed above as serious, stop taking the medication and seek immediate medical attention.**


**How it Works:**

Disulfiram inhibits the enzyme aldehyde dehydrogenase (ALDH), which plays a crucial role in metabolizing acetaldehyde, a toxic byproduct of alcohol breakdown. When an individual taking disulfiram consumes alcohol, acetaldehyde accumulates in the body, resulting in a range of unpleasant symptoms, including flushing, nausea, vomiting, headache, and palpitations.  This unpleasant reaction serves as a powerful deterrent to alcohol consumption.


**Precautions:**

*   **Contraindications:** Disulfiram should not be used by individuals with severe liver or kidney disease, heart conditions, or a history of psychosis. It should also not be used in patients with severe neurological or psychiatric disorders.
*   **Drug Interactions:** Disulfiram interacts with numerous medications, including metronidazole, isoniazid, and warfarin.  It's crucial to inform your doctor of all medications, supplements, and herbal remedies being taken before starting disulfiram.
*   **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:** Disulfiram should be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding.
*   **Older Adults:** Older adults may be more susceptible to side effects and require careful monitoring.


**FAQs:**

*   **Q: How long does it take to work?** A:  The deterrent effect starts immediately upon alcohol consumption while on the medication.  It doesn't prevent craving.

*   **Q: Can I drink a little alcohol while on disulfiram?** A: No. Even small amounts of alcohol can cause a severe reaction.

*   **Q: How long should I take disulfiram?** A: The duration of treatment varies depending on individual circumstances and is determined by your physician. It's usually part of a long-term plan for managing alcohol dependence.

*   **Q: How should I store disulfiram?** A: Store at room temperature, away from moisture and heat.  Keep out of reach of children.

*   **Q: What should I do if I accidentally take too much disulfiram?** A: Contact your doctor or seek immediate medical attention.
