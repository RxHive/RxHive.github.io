# Streptokinase

**Generic Name:** Streptokinase

**Usage:**

Streptokinase is a thrombolytic agent, meaning it dissolves blood clots. Its primary use is in the treatment of acute myocardial infarction (heart attack).  By breaking down the blood clot blocking blood flow to the heart muscle, it helps to limit the damage caused by the heart attack and improve the chances of survival and recovery.  

Beyond heart attacks, streptokinase has also been used, although less frequently now, in the treatment of other thromboembolic conditions, such as:

* **Pulmonary embolism:** A blood clot in the lungs.
* **Deep vein thrombosis (DVT):** A blood clot in a deep vein, usually in the leg.
* **Acute ischemic stroke:** A stroke caused by a blood clot blocking blood flow to the brain.  (Note:  Tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) is now the preferred treatment for ischemic stroke.)

The use of streptokinase in these conditions is often considered based on individual patient factors and the availability of alternative treatments.  Its use outside of heart attack is becoming less common due to the availability of newer, more effective, and potentially safer thrombolytic agents.

**Dosage:**

Streptokinase is administered intravenously (IV), meaning it's injected directly into a vein. Dosage varies greatly depending on the specific condition being treated and the patient's weight and other health factors.  Precise dosing regimens are determined by a physician and are not something that can be generalized here.  There are no oral or syrup forms of streptokinase.  It's exclusively administered intravenously under strict medical supervision. Children's dosing is even more complex and is strictly determined on a case-by-case basis by the attending physician.

**Side Effects:**

* **Common Side Effects:**  Bleeding (at injection site or internally), nausea, vomiting, fever, allergic reactions (rash, itching, hives), headache.
* **Serious (but less common) Side Effects:**  Severe bleeding (internal hemorrhage), stroke, anaphylaxis (severe allergic reaction), cardiac arrhythmias.

**Important Note:**  If you experience any side effects, especially those listed as serious, seek immediate medical attention.

**How it Works:**

Streptokinase activates plasminogen, a protein in the blood, converting it into plasmin. Plasmin is an enzyme that breaks down fibrin, the protein that forms the meshwork of a blood clot. By activating plasmin, streptokinase helps dissolve the blood clot, restoring blood flow to the affected area.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:**  Streptokinase should not be used in individuals with a history of allergic reactions to streptokinase or other streptococcal products, active internal bleeding, recent surgery, severe uncontrolled hypertension, or a history of stroke.  Pregnancy and breastfeeding should be carefully considered, as well.
* **Drug Interactions:**  Streptokinase can interact with numerous other medications, including anticoagulants (blood thinners).  It's crucial to inform your doctor of all medications you are taking.
* **Specific Warnings:**  Older adults and individuals with liver or kidney disease may be at increased risk of bleeding complications.  Careful monitoring is necessary for these patients.  Pregnant and breastfeeding women require special considerations and discussions with their physician before considering streptokinase.

**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does it take for streptokinase to work?** A: The effects of streptokinase can be seen relatively quickly, with clot lysis beginning within minutes of administration, however, the full therapeutic effect may take several hours.
* **Q: How is streptokinase stored?** A: Streptokinase requires specific storage conditions, usually refrigeration.  Always follow the instructions provided by your pharmacist or doctor.
* **Q: Is streptokinase safe?** A:  While streptokinase can be effective, it carries a risk of serious bleeding.  The benefits must be carefully weighed against these risks by a medical professional.
* **Q: Are there alternatives to streptokinase?** A: Yes, other thrombolytic agents like tissue plasminogen activator (tPA) are often preferred, particularly for stroke.  Your doctor will determine the best treatment option for your specific situation.
