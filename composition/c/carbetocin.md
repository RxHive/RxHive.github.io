# Carbetocin

**Generic Name: Carbetocin**

**Usage:**

Carbetocin is primarily used to prevent postpartum hemorrhage (PPH), a serious complication of childbirth characterized by excessive bleeding.  It works by stimulating the uterus to contract, helping to clamp down on blood vessels and reduce bleeding.  While its primary use is in preventing PPH after vaginal or Cesarean delivery, research is exploring its potential use in other areas, though these are not yet widely established clinical applications.

**Dosage:**

Carbetocin is typically administered intravenously (IV) as a single injection.  The standard adult dose is usually 100 mcg given immediately after delivery of the placenta.  There are no established pediatric dosages for carbetocin as its use is specifically focused on postpartum hemorrhage in adult women following childbirth.  The dosage may be adjusted based on the individual's clinical situation and at the discretion of the physician; however, variations in dosage are uncommon.  Carbetocin is not available in oral (tablet or syrup) forms.

**Side Effects:**

* **Common:** Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, headache, hypertension (high blood pressure),  uterine hyperstimulation (excessive contractions).
* **Less Common but Serious:**  Severe hypertension, seizures, thromboembolic events (blood clots), allergic reactions (rash, itching, swelling, difficulty breathing).


**Important Note:** If you experience any adverse effects, especially severe ones, seek immediate medical attention.


**How it Works:**

Carbetocin is a synthetic analogue of oxytocin, a naturally occurring hormone in the body that plays a crucial role in uterine contractions during labor and after delivery.  It acts by binding to oxytocin receptors in the uterus, triggering sustained uterine contractions that help control postpartum bleeding. Essentially, it mimics the body's natural mechanism for stopping bleeding after childbirth, but with a longer duration of effect compared to oxytocin.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Carbetocin should not be used in women with known hypersensitivity (allergy) to carbetocin or similar medications.  It is also generally avoided in cases of severe pre-eclampsia or eclampsia.
* **Drug Interactions:** Potential interactions with other medications are possible, but specific interactions are not well-documented.  It's vital to inform your healthcare provider of all medications you are taking, including over-the-counter drugs and herbal supplements.
* **Pregnant Women:**  Carbetocin is only used *after* childbirth to prevent postpartum hemorrhage; it is not used during pregnancy.
* **Children & Older Adults:** Carbetocin is not typically used in children or older adults, as its use is highly specific to postpartum hemorrhage in adult women who have recently given birth.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does the effect of carbetocin last?** A: The effects of carbetocin are longer-lasting than oxytocin, typically persisting for several hours.

* **Q: Can I store carbetocin at home?** A: No. Carbetocin is a medication administered in a hospital setting by trained medical professionals. It is not available for home storage.

* **Q: Is carbetocin safe?** A:  Like all medications, carbetocin carries potential risks. However, its benefits in preventing serious postpartum hemorrhage often outweigh the risks when used appropriately under medical supervision.

* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Carbetocin is typically administered as a single dose immediately after delivery; therefore, missing a dose is not applicable. Any concerns regarding its administration should be addressed with the attending physician.
