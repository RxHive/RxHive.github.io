# Capreomycin

**Generic Name: Capreomycin**

**Usage:**

Capreomycin is an aminoglycoside antibiotic used primarily to treat tuberculosis (TB), specifically in cases resistant to other first-line TB medications like rifampicin, isoniazid, pyrazinamide, and ethambutol.  It's typically used in combination with other anti-TB drugs as part of a multi-drug regimen, rather than as a standalone treatment. This approach is crucial to combat drug-resistant strains of *Mycobacterium tuberculosis*, the bacteria that cause TB.  While its primary use is for treating drug-resistant TB, it may also be considered in certain situations involving extensively drug-resistant (XDR) TB, although treatment strategies for XDR-TB are highly complex and individualized.  The decision to use capreomycin is always made by a physician specializing in infectious diseases, taking into account the patient's specific condition and resistance patterns.


**Dosage:**

Capreomycin is administered *only by injection*, typically intramuscularly (into the muscle).  There's no oral or other form of this medication. Dosage is highly individualized and determined by factors such as the severity of the infection, the patient's renal function (kidney health), and their overall health.  A physician will carefully monitor kidney function through regular blood tests because capreomycin can affect the kidneys.  

* **Adults:**  The typical dose ranges from 1 gram to 1.5 grams given once or twice weekly, but this can vary significantly.
* **Children:**  Dosage in children is calculated based on weight and renal function and should only be determined by a physician experienced in treating pediatric TB.

**Side Effects:**

* **Common:**  Nausea, vomiting, dizziness, headache, rash, injection site pain and redness.
* **Less Common but Serious:**  Hearing loss (ototoxicity), kidney damage (nephrotoxicity), neuromuscular blockade (muscle weakness), allergic reactions (ranging from mild skin reactions to severe anaphylaxis).

**Important Note:**  Any of these side effects, especially hearing loss or kidney problems, warrant immediate medical attention.  Regular monitoring of hearing and kidney function is essential during capreomycin treatment.


**How it Works:**

Capreomycin is a bacteriocidal antibiotic, meaning it kills bacteria.  It works by binding to the bacterial ribosome, a crucial cellular structure responsible for protein synthesis. By interfering with this process, capreomycin prevents the *Mycobacterium tuberculosis* bacteria from producing the proteins necessary for their survival and replication, effectively halting their growth and leading to their death.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:**  Capreomycin should not be used in patients with severe kidney disease or known hypersensitivity (allergy) to aminoglycoside antibiotics.
* **Drug Interactions:**  Capreomycin may interact with other medications, especially other nephrotoxic drugs (those that can damage the kidneys) and neuromuscular blocking agents.  It is crucial to inform your doctor about all medications you are taking.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  The use of capreomycin during pregnancy and breastfeeding should be carefully considered by a physician, weighing the risks and benefits.
* **Older Adults and Children:**  Both older adults and children are at increased risk of developing side effects from capreomycin due to their potentially decreased renal function and different physiological responses.  Close monitoring is essential.

**Critical Safety Measure:**  Regular monitoring of kidney function (through blood tests) and hearing (through audiometry) are critical during capreomycin therapy to detect and manage potential side effects.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long do I need to take capreomycin?**  A: The duration of treatment depends on the severity of the infection and the patient's response. It is usually part of a prolonged multi-drug regimen determined by the treating physician.
* **Q: Can I stop taking capreomycin if I feel better?** A: No, it's crucial to complete the entire course of treatment prescribed by your doctor, even if you feel better.  Stopping early can lead to drug resistance.
* **Q: How should I store capreomycin?** A: Follow the specific storage instructions provided by your pharmacist.  Generally, injectable medications should be stored according to the manufacturer's guidelines.
* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Contact your doctor or pharmacist immediately to discuss the next steps.  Do not try to adjust the dose yourself.
