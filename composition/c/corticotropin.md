# Corticotropin

**Generic Name:** Corticotropin

**Usage:**

Corticotropin, also known as adrenocorticotropic hormone (ACTH), is a hormone naturally produced by the pituitary gland.  Medically, it's used primarily to diagnose conditions affecting the adrenal glands.  This involves a stimulation test where corticotropin is administered, and subsequent cortisol levels are measured to assess adrenal function.  This is crucial in diagnosing conditions like Addison's disease (adrenal insufficiency), where the adrenal glands don't produce enough cortisol.  Secondary uses include treating infantile spasms (a severe type of epilepsy in infants) and multiple sclerosis (MS) in certain cases, though its efficacy in these conditions is debated and its use is often considered a last resort after other treatments have failed.  It's important to note that corticotropin's use in treating MS and infantile spasms isn't as widespread or established as its diagnostic role in adrenal insufficiency.

**Dosage:**

Dosage of corticotropin varies significantly depending on the condition being treated and the individual's age and weight. It is almost always administered by injection (intravenous, intramuscular, or subcutaneous). There are no oral forms.  A physician will determine the appropriate dose and frequency.  For diagnostic tests, the dosage is typically standardized,  whereas therapeutic dosages for conditions like infantile spasms are individually adjusted based on the child's response and clinical picture. Specific dosing guidelines are complex and should only be determined and monitored by a qualified healthcare professional.

**Side Effects:**

* **Common:**  Headache, nausea, vomiting, increased blood pressure, flushing.
* **Less Common but Serious:**  Fluid retention, increased potassium excretion (hypokalemia),  worsening of existing diabetes, mood changes (including psychosis in rare cases), increased risk of infection.  In rare cases, very high doses can lead to adrenal suppression (the body's natural production of cortisol is reduced).

* **Important Note:**  Any new or worsening symptoms should be reported to a doctor immediately.


**How it Works:**

Corticotropin stimulates the adrenal glands to produce cortisol and other corticosteroids.  In diagnostic tests, this helps assess the adrenal glands' responsiveness.  In therapeutic use (infantile spasms, some MS cases), the increased cortisol levels are believed to have an anti-inflammatory and potentially anticonvulsant effect.  The precise mechanisms of action in these therapeutic contexts aren't fully understood.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Corticotropin should not be used in individuals with hypersensitivity (allergy) to it.  Caution is needed in patients with various conditions, including diabetes, heart failure, hypertension, psychosis, and peptic ulcers.  A comprehensive medical history is essential before corticotropin administration.
* **Drug Interactions:** Corticotropin can interact with other medications. It's crucial to inform your healthcare provider about all medications, supplements, and herbal remedies being taken.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  The use of corticotropin during pregnancy and breastfeeding requires careful assessment of the benefits versus risks by a healthcare professional.
* **Children and Older Adults:**  Dosage adjustments may be necessary for children and older adults based on their age, weight, and overall health. Close monitoring is crucial.

**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does it take for corticotropin to work?** A: This depends on the use.  In diagnostic tests, the effects are seen relatively quickly (within hours). For therapeutic uses, the effects may take longer to become apparent.
* **Q: How is corticotropin stored?** A:  Storage instructions vary depending on the specific formulation.  Always follow the storage instructions provided by the manufacturer.  Generally, it requires refrigeration.
* **Q: Can I stop taking corticotropin suddenly?** A:  No, especially if it's being used therapeutically.  Stopping abruptly can have adverse consequences. It's crucial to follow your doctor's instructions for tapering off the medication if necessary.
* **Q: Is corticotropin addictive?** A:  Physical dependence can develop with prolonged therapeutic use at high doses.  Withdrawal should be carefully managed under medical supervision to prevent adverse reactions.
* **Q: What are the long-term effects of using corticotropin?** A:  Long-term use can increase the risk of various side effects, such as those listed above, with some becoming more pronounced over time. It's therefore essential to minimize long-term use unless absolutely necessary and carefully monitored by a physician.
