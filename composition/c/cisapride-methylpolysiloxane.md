# Cisapride + Methylpolysiloxane

**Generic Name: Cisapride + Methylpolysiloxane**

This combination is not a currently marketed pharmaceutical product.  Cisapride and methylpolysiloxane are used separately in different medical contexts.  Cisapride, a prokinetic agent, was once used to treat gastrointestinal motility disorders.  However, due to serious cardiac side effects, it has been withdrawn from most markets, including India. Methylpolysiloxane is a silicone-based compound used as an antifoaming agent in various applications, including some pharmaceutical formulations.  Therefore, a combination of these two substances would require careful consideration and likely wouldn't be approved without extensive testing and regulatory clearance.  The information below reflects the properties of *each component individually* and should not be interpreted as a description of a currently available combination drug.

**Usage:**

* **Cisapride (Historically):**  Primarily used to treat gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), symptoms of gastroparesis (delayed stomach emptying), and certain types of dyspepsia (indigestion). It accelerated the movement of food through the digestive tract.  *Note: Cisapride is largely unavailable due to safety concerns.*

* **Methylpolysiloxane:** Primarily used as an antifoaming agent, often included in over-the-counter medications to reduce gas and bloating. It is not a drug that treats specific medical conditions in its own right but improves the delivery and efficacy of other drugs.


**Dosage:**

* **Cisapride (Historically):**  Dosage varied greatly depending on the specific condition and patient factors.  Specific dosage information is not provided here as the drug is largely unavailable.  A healthcare professional should always determine the appropriate dose.

* **Methylpolysiloxane:** The dosage of methylpolysiloxane depends entirely on the formulation of the drug.  This information is not available without a specific product name and concentration.


**Side Effects:**

* **Cisapride (Historically):**
    * **Common:** Diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, headache, abdominal cramps.
    * **Serious (Less Common but potentially life-threatening):**  Prolonged QT interval (a heart rhythm abnormality that can lead to life-threatening arrhythmias), torsades de pointes (a type of irregular heartbeat), cardiac arrest.

* **Methylpolysiloxane:** Generally considered safe and well-tolerated at typical levels.  Side effects are rare.

**How it Works:**

* **Cisapride (Historically):**  Cisapride increased the activity of the gastrointestinal tract by affecting the release of serotonin, a neurotransmitter that helps regulate gut motility. This increased movement helped speed up digestion and reduce symptoms of GERD and gastroparesis.

* **Methylpolysiloxane:** Acts solely as a physical antifoaming agent. It breaks up and disperses gas bubbles in the digestive tract, preventing gas build-up and bloating. It does not have any systemic pharmacological effects.


**Popular Brand Names:**

There are no commonly available brand names for a combination of cisapride and methylpolysiloxane.  Cisapride, previously available under various brand names, is no longer widely marketed.


**Precautions:**

* **Cisapride (Historically):** Should not be used by people with prolonged QT syndrome, heart rhythm abnormalities, or those taking certain medications that prolong the QT interval.  Cisapride was contraindicated in pregnancy and breastfeeding.

* **Methylpolysiloxane:**  Generally safe, but those with known silicone allergies should avoid products containing it.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: Is cisapride still available?** A: Due to serious cardiac risks, cisapride is no longer widely available in most countries.
* **Q: Is methylpolysiloxane a drug?** A:  No, it's primarily an excipient (an inactive ingredient) in formulations, serving as an antifoaming agent.
* **Q: How should I store medications containing methylpolysiloxane?** A: Follow the storage instructions on the specific product label.  Most such products need to be stored at room temperature.
* **Q: Can I take medications containing methylpolysiloxane with other drugs?** A: Check with your healthcare provider or pharmacist for potential drug interactions.  Although methylpolysiloxane itself is largely inert, it might be included in a medication that does interact with other drugs.
