# Glucagon

**Generic Name: Glucagon**

**Usage:**

Glucagon is a hormone naturally produced by the pancreas.  Its primary medical use is in the emergency treatment of severe hypoglycemia (low blood sugar), particularly in individuals with diabetes who are using insulin or certain other diabetes medications.  A dangerously low blood sugar level can lead to serious complications, including seizures, coma, and even death.  Glucagon rapidly raises blood glucose levels by stimulating the liver to release stored glucose into the bloodstream.

While its primary role is in emergency hypoglycemia, glucagon may also have secondary uses in specific diagnostic procedures, such as imaging studies of the pancreas. Its use in these contexts is much less frequent and requires careful medical supervision.

**Dosage:**

Glucagon is typically administered via injection, usually intramuscularly or subcutaneously (under the skin).  Dosage is determined by a healthcare professional based on the individual's age, weight, and the severity of the hypoglycemic event.  There isn't a standard "child" or "adult" dose in the way there is for many other medications.  The dosage is calculated according to weight and the specific glucagon product.  

The medication is not typically available in tablet or syrup form due to its instability when ingested orally.  Detailed instructions for administering glucagon injections are always provided with the medication and should be carefully followed.

**Side Effects:**

* Nausea and vomiting
* Dizziness
* Headache
* Allergic reactions (rash, itching, swelling)
* Flushing (redness of the skin)
* Anxiety
* Hypotension (low blood pressure)

**Serious but less common side effects:** include severe allergic reactions (anaphylaxis) requiring immediate medical attention.  

**Note:** If any adverse effects occur, immediately contact a healthcare provider for guidance.


**How it Works:**

Glucagon works by binding to specific receptors on liver cells. This binding triggers a cascade of events that ultimately lead to the breakdown of glycogen (stored glucose) into glucose, which is then released into the bloodstream. This process rapidly increases blood glucose levels, counteracting the effects of hypoglycemia.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Glucagon should not be used in individuals with known allergy to glucagon or any of its components.
* **Drug Interactions:**  Some medications may interfere with glucagon's effectiveness.  Healthcare providers must be informed of all medications a patient is taking.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:** The use of glucagon during pregnancy and breastfeeding should be carefully considered and discussed with a healthcare professional, weighing the benefits against potential risks.
* **Older Adults and Children:** Dosage adjustments may be necessary for both older adults and children, determined by a healthcare professional based on individual needs.
* **Pheochromocytoma:** Patients with pheochromocytoma (a tumor of the adrenal gland) require special precautions as glucagon can potentially exacerbate certain symptoms.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does it take for glucagon to work?**  A:  Effects are usually seen within 10-20 minutes of injection.
* **Q: Can I store glucagon at room temperature?** A:  Glucagon should be stored according to the manufacturer's instructions, typically refrigerated. Check your product's packaging for specific storage guidelines.
* **Q:  What should I do if I accidentally inject too much glucagon?** A: Immediately seek medical attention.
* **Q: Can I use glucagon to prevent hypoglycemia?** A: No, glucagon is for emergency treatment of already occurring hypoglycemia, not for prevention.
* **Q:  Is glucagon safe for long-term use?** A: Glucagon is not intended for regular or long-term use.  It is primarily an emergency medication.
