# Nicotinic acid

**Generic Name:** Nicotinic Acid (also known as Niacin)

**Usage:**

Nicotinic acid is primarily used to treat **pellagra**, a serious disease caused by a deficiency of niacin (vitamin B3).  Pellagra symptoms include skin rashes, diarrhea, and dementia.  Nicotinic acid effectively replenishes niacin levels, resolving these symptoms.

Beyond treating deficiency, nicotinic acid also plays a significant role in managing **high cholesterol** levels.  Specifically, it's effective at lowering LDL ("bad") cholesterol and triglycerides, while also increasing HDL ("good") cholesterol. This makes it a valuable addition to other cholesterol-lowering therapies for individuals who haven't achieved their target cholesterol levels with diet and lifestyle changes alone. It's important to note that  it is usually *not* the first-line treatment for high cholesterol. Doctors often prescribe statins first.  Nicotinic acid might be considered as an *adjunctive* therapy, meaning it's used alongside other treatments.


**Dosage:**

Dosage of nicotinic acid varies greatly depending on the reason for its use.  A healthcare professional will determine the appropriate dosage based on individual needs and health status.  

* **For pellagra:** The dosage will be determined by the severity of the deficiency and is typically administered under direct medical supervision.  It might involve intravenous administration initially, followed by oral supplementation.

* **For high cholesterol:** Dosages generally start low and are gradually increased to find the most effective and tolerable amount.  Oral forms (tablets, extended-release tablets) are most common. Typical starting doses for cholesterol management are much lower than those used to treat pellagra.  Extended-release formulations are often preferred to minimize flushing (see side effects).

* **Children:**  Nicotinic acid use in children is typically limited to treating pellagra under close medical supervision.  Dosage will be carefully adjusted based on the child's age, weight, and the severity of the deficiency.

**Important Note:** Never self-medicate with nicotinic acid.  Always consult a doctor to determine the appropriate dosage and ensure it's safe for you.


**Side Effects:**

* **Common:** Flushing (redness and warmth in the skin, especially the face and neck), itching, nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, headache.  These are usually temporary and can be minimized by taking a lower dose or using extended-release formulations.
* **Less Common but Serious:** Liver damage (hepatotoxicity), elevated blood sugar levels (hyperglycemia), gout, stomach ulcers.

**If you experience any adverse effects, especially those listed as less common or serious, stop taking nicotinic acid and consult your doctor immediately.**


**How it Works:**

Nicotinic acid is a form of vitamin B3.  In the body, it plays a crucial role in various metabolic processes.  For cholesterol management, its exact mechanism isn't fully understood, but it's believed to influence the production and breakdown of fats in the liver, leading to reductions in LDL cholesterol and triglycerides, and an increase in HDL cholesterol. In pellagra, it directly addresses the niacin deficiency, allowing the body to function normally again.


**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Nicotinic acid is contraindicated in individuals with active liver disease or a history of severe liver damage.
* **Drug Interactions:**  Nicotinic acid can interact with certain medications, including blood thinners (anticoagulants), diabetes medications, and some cholesterol-lowering drugs. It's essential to inform your doctor about all medications and supplements you're taking.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:** The safety of nicotinic acid during pregnancy and breastfeeding is not fully established. Consult your doctor before using it if you are pregnant, breastfeeding, or planning to become pregnant.
* **Older Adults:** Older adults may be more sensitive to the side effects of nicotinic acid, requiring careful monitoring and potentially lower doses.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: Can I buy nicotinic acid over-the-counter?**  A:  While some forms of nicotinic acid are available over-the-counter as supplements, it's crucial to consult a doctor before using it, especially for medical conditions like high cholesterol.  The appropriate dosage and monitoring are vital for safety and effectiveness.

* **Q: How should I store nicotinic acid?** A: Store nicotinic acid in a cool, dry place, away from direct sunlight and moisture, as directed on the product label.

* **Q: How long does it take to see results?** A: The time it takes to see results varies depending on the condition being treated and the individual's response. For high cholesterol, it may take several weeks to see significant changes.  For pellagra, improvements are generally seen more quickly with appropriate treatment.

* **Q: Is nicotinic acid safe for long-term use?** A:  Long-term use requires careful medical monitoring, especially for potential liver effects. Your doctor will assess your need for continued use and adjust the dosage as needed.
