# Raloxifene

**Generic Name: Raloxifene**

**Usage:**

Raloxifene is a selective estrogen receptor modulator (SERM).  Its primary use is in the prevention and treatment of osteoporosis in postmenopausal women.  This means it helps to strengthen bones and reduce the risk of fractures.  Specifically, it works by mimicking the beneficial effects of estrogen on bones while avoiding many of the negative effects estrogen can have on other parts of the body, such as the uterus and breasts.

A secondary use of raloxifene is in reducing the risk of invasive breast cancer in postmenopausal women at high risk.  It's important to note that raloxifene is *not* a treatment for existing breast cancer.  Its role is preventative.


**Dosage:**

The typical adult dosage of raloxifene is 60 mg once daily, taken with food.  This is usually administered as a tablet.  There are no pediatric formulations of raloxifene, and it is not approved for use in children.  Dosage adjustments based on age, weight, or severity of osteoporosis are not typically made, but individual patient needs should always be discussed with a healthcare provider.  The medication should be taken consistently as prescribed for optimal effectiveness.  Never adjust the dosage on your own.


**Side Effects:**

Common side effects of raloxifene can include:

* Hot flashes
* Leg cramps
* Headaches
* Flu-like symptoms
* Nausea
* Swelling in the legs or ankles (edema)
* Vaginal bleeding or discharge (though this is less common than with other hormone therapies)


Less common, but more serious side effects, include:

* Blood clots (deep vein thrombosis or pulmonary embolism) - Seek immediate medical attention if you experience sudden chest pain, shortness of breath, or leg pain/swelling.
* Stroke
* Retinal artery occlusion (reduced blood flow to the retina)


If you experience any concerning side effects,  contact your doctor or pharmacist immediately.


**How it Works:**

Raloxifene works by binding to estrogen receptors in the body.  In some tissues, like bone, it acts like estrogen, promoting bone growth and density.  However, in other tissues, such as the uterus and breast, it acts as an estrogen *blocker*, preventing the estrogen-related stimulation that can contribute to uterine cancer or breast cancer growth. This selective action is what makes it a SERM.


**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Raloxifene should not be used by women who are pregnant, breastfeeding, or who have a history of blood clots, stroke, or unexplained vaginal bleeding.  It's also not suitable for those with severe liver disease.
* **Drug Interactions:** Raloxifene can interact with certain medications.  Always inform your doctor or pharmacist of all medications, supplements, and herbal remedies you are taking.
* **Vulnerable Groups:**  As mentioned, raloxifene is contraindicated in pregnant and breastfeeding women.  Older adults should be monitored closely for side effects, particularly those related to blood clots.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: Can I stop taking raloxifene if I feel better?** A: No, you should continue taking raloxifene as prescribed by your doctor, even if you feel better.  Stopping prematurely may reduce its effectiveness.
* **Q: How should I store raloxifene?** A: Store raloxifene at room temperature, away from moisture and direct sunlight.
* **Q: Is raloxifene a hormone replacement therapy (HRT)?** A: No, raloxifene is a selective estrogen receptor modulator (SERM), not a hormone replacement therapy. While it has some estrogen-like effects, it doesn't replace estrogen completely and has a different mechanism of action.
* **Q: What if I miss a dose?** A: Take the missed dose as soon as you remember, unless it is almost time for your next dose.  Do not double the dose.
* **Q:  Will raloxifene increase my risk of endometrial cancer?** A:  Unlike some hormone replacement therapies, raloxifene is associated with a *lower* risk of endometrial cancer.
