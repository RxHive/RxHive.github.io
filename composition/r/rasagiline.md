# Rasagiline

**Generic Name: Rasagiline**

**Usage:**

Rasagiline is primarily used to treat Parkinson's disease.  Its main role is as an adjunct therapy, meaning it's used *in addition* to other Parkinson's medications, particularly levodopa.  It helps improve the motor symptoms of Parkinson's, such as tremors, rigidity, slow movement (bradykinesia), and postural instability.  While not a first-line treatment, it can help manage symptoms and potentially delay the need for higher doses of other medications.  Some studies also suggest a potential neuroprotective effect, meaning it might help protect brain cells from further damage, although more research is needed to confirm this fully.  It's important to note that rasagiline does not cure Parkinson's disease.


**Dosage:**

The usual starting dose for adults with Parkinson's disease is 1 mg once daily.  A healthcare professional may gradually increase this to 2 mg once daily depending on individual response and tolerance.  Rasagiline is typically available as a tablet. There are no established pediatric dosages, and it's not generally recommended for use in children.  Dosage adjustments based on age, weight, or specific disease severity are typically made under the close supervision of a physician.  Never alter your prescribed dosage without consulting your doctor.


**Side Effects:**

* **Common side effects:**  Nausea, dizziness, insomnia, headache, constipation, and dry mouth.  These are usually mild and may lessen over time.
* **Less common, but serious side effects:**  Orthostatic hypotension (a sudden drop in blood pressure upon standing), hallucinations, confusion, and serotonin syndrome (a rare but potentially life-threatening condition characterized by high fever, agitation, and muscle rigidity).
* **Important Note:**  If you experience any unusual or concerning side effects, stop taking rasagiline and contact your healthcare provider immediately.


**How it Works:**

Rasagiline is a selective, irreversible inhibitor of monoamine oxidase type B (MAO-B).  MAO-B is an enzyme that breaks down dopamine, a neurotransmitter crucial for movement control.  By inhibiting MAO-B, rasagiline helps to increase the levels of dopamine in the brain. This increased dopamine helps alleviate the motor symptoms associated with Parkinson's disease.


**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:**  Rasagiline should not be used with certain other medications, particularly those that are also MAO inhibitors or strong serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs)  or serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs).  There's a significant risk of serious interactions.  Your physician must carefully review your entire medication list.
* **Interactions:**  Many drug interactions are possible.  Always inform your doctor and pharmacist of all medications, supplements, and herbal remedies you are taking.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  The safety of rasagiline during pregnancy and breastfeeding has not been fully established.  Discuss the risks and benefits with your doctor before using rasagiline if you are pregnant, breastfeeding, or planning to become pregnant.
* **Older Adults:** Older adults may be more sensitive to the side effects of rasagiline, particularly orthostatic hypotension.  Close monitoring is usually recommended.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does it take for rasagiline to work?** A:  It may take several weeks to see a noticeable improvement in symptoms.
* **Q: Can I stop taking rasagiline suddenly?** A: No, never stop taking rasagiline abruptly without consulting your doctor.  Sudden discontinuation may lead to withdrawal symptoms.
* **Q: How should I store rasagiline?** A: Store rasagiline at room temperature, away from moisture and light.
* **Q:  Can I drink alcohol while taking rasagiline?** A:  It's advisable to limit alcohol consumption while taking rasagiline, as alcohol can worsen some side effects, such as dizziness. Always discuss this with your doctor.
* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Take the missed dose as soon as you remember, unless it is almost time for your next dose.  Do not double the dose to make up for a missed one.
