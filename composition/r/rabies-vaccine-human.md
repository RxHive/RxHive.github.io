# Rabies vaccine + Human

**Generic Name:** Rabies Vaccine + Human

**Usage:**

The rabies vaccine is used to prevent rabies, a deadly viral disease transmitted through the saliva of infected animals, typically through a bite.  Its primary use is pre-exposure prophylaxis (Pre-exposure prophylaxis, or PrEP) for individuals at high risk of rabies exposure, such as veterinarians, wildlife handlers, and laboratory workers who handle rabies viruses.  The vaccine is also crucial for post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP) following a potential rabies exposure, such as a bite or scratch from an animal suspected of having rabies. In PEP, the vaccine is given in conjunction with rabies immunoglobulin (RIG) to provide immediate and lasting protection.  While not a treatment for active rabies infection (once symptoms appear, the disease is almost always fatal), the vaccine is vital in preventing the disease from developing after exposure.


**Dosage:**

Dosage and administration vary depending on whether it's pre-exposure or post-exposure prophylaxis.  The vaccine is administered intramuscularly (IM) only.  

* **Pre-exposure prophylaxis (PrEP):**  A standard course typically involves a series of three injections given over a period of several weeks or months, with a booster dose given later. The specific schedule will be determined by the healthcare professional based on guidelines and risk factors.

* **Post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP):**  This requires a more intensive schedule, usually involving a series of injections administered over a shorter time frame, often alongside rabies immunoglobulin (RIG). The exact regimen is determined by the healthcare provider based on factors like the severity of the exposure (e.g., depth and location of the bite), the animal's rabies status (if known), and the patient's age and health status.  Children and adults will receive different doses based on their weight and age as determined by healthcare providers.


**Side Effects:**

* **Common:**  Pain, redness, or swelling at the injection site.  Headache, muscle aches, fatigue, nausea, and fever are also possible.  These are usually mild and temporary.
* **Less Common but Serious:**  Allergic reactions (ranging from mild rash to anaphylaxis),  neurological symptoms (such as Guillain-Barré syndrome, although extremely rare).
* **Note:** If any significant or concerning side effects develop, seek immediate medical attention.


**How it Works:**

The rabies vaccine contains inactivated (killed) rabies virus.  When injected, the vaccine stimulates the body's immune system to produce antibodies against the rabies virus. These antibodies provide protection against future infection.  If exposed to rabies after vaccination, the body's pre-existing antibodies and immune cells will quickly neutralize the virus, preventing the disease from developing.


**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:**  Individuals with a known severe allergic reaction to any component of the vaccine should not receive it.
* **Drug Interactions:**  Inform your healthcare provider about all medications, including over-the-counter drugs and supplements, you are currently taking.
* **Vulnerable Groups:**  Pregnant or breastfeeding women should discuss the risks and benefits with their healthcare provider.  Caution should also be exercised in individuals with weakened immune systems.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does the rabies vaccine provide protection?** A:  The duration of protection varies depending on the vaccination schedule (PrEP vs. PEP) and individual immune response. For PrEP, booster doses may be necessary to maintain immunity.
* **Q: What should I do if I'm bitten by an animal?** A:  Seek immediate medical attention.  The sooner you receive PEP, the better your chances of preventing rabies. Thoroughly wash the wound with soap and water.
* **Q: Is the rabies vaccine safe?** A: The rabies vaccine is highly effective and generally safe.  However, as with any vaccine, side effects are possible, as outlined above.
* **Q: How is the vaccine stored?** A:  The vaccine must be stored according to the manufacturer's instructions, typically refrigerated.  Improper storage can affect the vaccine's potency.
* **Q: Can I get rabies from the vaccine?** A: No, the vaccine contains inactivated (killed) rabies virus and cannot cause rabies.
