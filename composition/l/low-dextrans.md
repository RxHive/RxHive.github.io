# Low Dextrans

**Generic Name: Low Dextrans**

**Usage:**

Low-molecular-weight dextrans (low dextrans) are primarily used as plasma volume expanders.  This means they're administered intravenously to increase the volume of blood plasma, effectively boosting blood pressure in situations where this is critically low.  Their primary use is in the treatment of hypovolemic shock, a condition where severe blood loss leads to inadequate blood supply to vital organs. This can result from trauma, surgery, burns, or severe dehydration.  Low dextrans are also sometimes used as adjuvant therapy (supportive treatment) in situations where blood viscosity (thickness) needs to be reduced to improve blood flow. This secondary use is less common and often considered on a case-by-case basis by medical professionals.


**Dosage:**

Dosage of low dextrans varies significantly depending on the patient's condition, weight, and the severity of blood loss.  It's exclusively administered intravenously by a healthcare professional, never self-administered. There are no standard oral or other forms of low dextran medication available.  Dosage is determined by a physician based on factors such as blood pressure, heart rate, and ongoing fluid loss.  Specific instructions are tailored to each patient and monitoring is crucial during administration to avoid complications.  There are no specific pediatric or geriatric dosing guidelines beyond those dictated by individual patient needs and careful monitoring.


**Side Effects:**

* **Common:**  Mild allergic reactions (rash, itching, hives), headache, flushing, nausea.
* **Less Common but Serious:**  Anaphylaxis (severe allergic reaction), fluid overload (leading to pulmonary edema – fluid in the lungs), bleeding tendencies (due to interference with platelet function), kidney problems (especially in patients with pre-existing renal impairment).


**How it Works:**

Low dextrans are composed of glucose molecules linked together in a specific way.  Their low molecular weight allows them to remain in the bloodstream longer than higher molecular weight dextrans.  Because they are osmotically active, they draw fluid from the tissues into the bloodstream, increasing plasma volume and, subsequently, blood pressure. They also have some properties that may help reduce blood viscosity. This combined effect improves blood flow and oxygen delivery to vital organs.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Low dextrans should not be used in patients with known allergies to dextran, severe heart failure, or severe kidney disease.
* **Drug Interactions:**  Dextrans may interact with certain medications, affecting their effectiveness or increasing the risk of side effects.  Complete disclosure of all medications being taken is crucial.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:** The safety of low dextrans during pregnancy and breastfeeding has not been fully established; therefore, its use should be carefully considered and only administered under strict medical supervision.
* **Elderly and Children:**  Special caution is advised in elderly patients and children due to the potential for increased sensitivity to side effects and the presence of pre-existing conditions.  Close monitoring is essential.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does it take for low dextran to work?** A: The effects of low dextran infusion are usually seen relatively quickly, as the increase in plasma volume is immediate.
* **Q: How is low dextran stored?** A: Storage instructions will vary by the specific brand; follow the guidelines provided on the product label and consult a pharmacist for correct storage practices.
* **Q: Can I take low dextran at home?** A: No. Low dextran is administered intravenously by trained medical personnel in a clinical setting.
* **Q: What should I do if I experience side effects?** A: Stop the infusion immediately and inform your healthcare provider of any adverse reactions.
* **Q: Is low dextran a substitute for blood transfusion?** A: No. While it increases plasma volume, it does not replace the red blood cells and other blood components lost during hemorrhaging.
