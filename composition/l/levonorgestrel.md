# Levonorgestrel

**Generic Name: Levonorgestrel**

**Usage:**

Levonorgestrel is primarily known as an emergency contraceptive, often referred to as the "morning-after pill."  It works to prevent pregnancy after unprotected sex or contraceptive failure.  This is its most common and widely understood use.  However, levonorgestrel also has other applications. It's a component in some combined oral contraceptives (birth control pills) where it works in conjunction with estrogen to prevent ovulation.  Furthermore, it's available in intrauterine systems (IUS), such as the Mirena IUD, providing long-term reversible contraception by thickening cervical mucus and preventing implantation.  In some cases, it may be used to treat certain hormonal imbalances, though this is less common.


**Dosage:**

Dosage varies considerably depending on the specific formulation and intended use.  

* **Emergency Contraception (Levonorgestrel Tablets):** Typically a single dose of 1.5mg is taken as soon as possible, but ideally within 72 hours (3 days) after unprotected sex.  Some formulations might require two tablets taken 12 hours apart.  Always follow the specific instructions on the packaging.

* **Combined Oral Contraceptives:** Dosage varies widely depending on the specific formulation and brand. The amount of levonorgestrel will be listed on the packaging and should be followed precisely as directed by the prescribing physician.

* **Intrauterine System (IUS):**  The dosage is not applicable here as it's a slow-release device implanted by a healthcare professional.


**Side Effects:**

* **Common:** Nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, fatigue, headache, dizziness, breast tenderness.

* **Less Common but Serious:**  Severe abdominal pain (may indicate ectopic pregnancy), heavy bleeding, allergic reactions (rash, itching, swelling).

* **Important Note:**  If you experience any severe or persistent side effects, seek immediate medical attention.


**How it Works:**

Levonorgestrel's primary mechanism as emergency contraception is believed to be primarily by preventing or delaying ovulation.  In other words, it stops the release of an egg from the ovary.  It may also thicken cervical mucus, making it harder for sperm to reach the egg, and potentially interfere with implantation (though this is less certain). In combined oral contraceptives, it acts in concert with estrogen to suppress ovulation and alter cervical mucus consistency. In the IUS, it provides a sustained, local release of the hormone within the uterus, creating a hostile environment for sperm and preventing implantation.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Levonorgestrel is contraindicated for individuals with known hypersensitivity to the drug. It should be used cautiously in women with a history of liver disease or blood clots.

* **Drug Interactions:**  Some medications may interact with levonorgestrel.  Always inform your doctor or pharmacist about all medications, supplements, or herbal remedies you are taking.

* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  Levonorgestrel should not be used as a regular form of contraception. While generally considered safe in breastfeeding women, it's crucial to consult a healthcare provider before use.  It is not for use during pregnancy.

* **Children:** Levonorgestrel is not intended for use in children.

* **Older Adults:**  The impact on older adults is not significantly different from that on other adults regarding emergency contraception, but it should still be used cautiously, with attention to any potential pre-existing conditions.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: Is levonorgestrel effective every time?** A: No, while it is highly effective when used correctly, it is not 100% effective in preventing pregnancy.

* **Q: How soon should I take it after unprotected sex?** A:  The sooner you take it after unprotected sex (ideally within 72 hours), the more effective it will be.

* **Q: Will levonorgestrel affect my future fertility?** A: No, levonorgestrel does not affect future fertility.

* **Q: How should I store levonorgestrel?** A: Store it as directed on the packaging, typically in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture.

* **Q: Is levonorgestrel safe to use repeatedly?** A: No, it's not intended for regular use. It's designed for emergency situations. Regular contraception methods should be considered for ongoing protection.
