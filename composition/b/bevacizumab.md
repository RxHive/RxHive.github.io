# Bevacizumab

**Generic Name: Bevacizumab**

**Usage:**

Bevacizumab is a targeted cancer therapy, primarily used to prevent the growth of new blood vessels that tumors need to survive and spread. This process is known as angiogenesis.  It's approved for several types of cancer, and its use often involves combination therapy with other cancer drugs.

Its primary use is in treating metastatic colorectal cancer (cancer that has spread to other parts of the body), and it's also frequently used in treating certain types of lung cancer (specifically non-small cell lung cancer), kidney cancer (renal cell carcinoma), cervical cancer, glioblastoma (a type of brain cancer), and ovarian cancer.  In some cases, bevacizumab may be used in other cancers, but this is usually decided on a case-by-case basis by oncologists. The specific type of cancer, its stage, and the patient's overall health all play a role in determining suitability.

**Dosage:**

Bevacizumab is administered intravenously (IV), meaning it's injected directly into a vein. There's no oral (tablet or syrup) form of this medication.  Dosage is highly individualized and determined by a physician based on factors like the type and stage of cancer, the patient's overall health, and response to treatment.  A typical dose for adults might range from 5 mg/kg to 10 mg/kg of body weight, given every two to three weeks.  Pediatric dosing is significantly different and must be carefully calculated by a pediatric oncologist based on age, weight, and specific cancer type.  The exact schedule and duration of treatment vary greatly depending on the patient’s condition and response to the medication.


**Side Effects:**

Common side effects can include:

* High blood pressure (hypertension)
* Bleeding (including nosebleeds, gastrointestinal bleeding)
* Fatigue
* Headaches
* Nausea and vomiting
* Protein in the urine (proteinuria)
* Mouth sores
* Infection
* Constipation
* Reduced blood cell counts (anemia, neutropenia, thrombocytopenia)

Less common, but serious side effects can include:

* Blood clots (thromboembolism)
* Perforation of the intestines (intestinal rupture)
* Severe allergic reactions
* Wound healing problems
* Heart failure


**Important Note:** Any side effect, even if seemingly minor, should be reported to your healthcare provider immediately.  Prompt attention to side effects can help prevent more serious complications.


**How it Works:**

Bevacizumab is a monoclonal antibody.  This means it's a laboratory-made protein that targets a specific protein found in the body called vascular endothelial growth factor (VEGF). VEGF plays a crucial role in the formation of new blood vessels. By binding to VEGF, bevacizumab blocks its activity, thus reducing the formation of new blood vessels that feed the tumor, essentially starving the cancer cells and slowing or halting tumor growth.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Bevacizumab is contraindicated (should not be used) in patients with uncontrolled hypertension, active gastrointestinal perforation, or significant bleeding disorders.
* **Drug Interactions:** Bevacizumab can interact with other medications, particularly those that affect blood clotting or blood pressure.  Full disclosure of all medications to your oncologist is crucial.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:** Bevacizumab can harm a developing fetus. It's crucial to avoid pregnancy during treatment and for several weeks afterward.  Breastfeeding should be discontinued during treatment.
* **Older Adults and Children:** Older adults may be more susceptible to certain side effects. Pediatric use requires careful consideration of potential risks and benefits under the close supervision of a pediatric oncologist.  Dosing adjustments may be needed in both cases.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long will I be on bevacizumab?** A: The duration of treatment depends on your individual response to therapy and is determined by your oncologist.
* **Q: How is bevacizumab stored?** A:  Specific storage instructions are provided by the manufacturer and should be strictly followed. Generally, it needs to be refrigerated.
* **Q:  What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Contact your healthcare provider immediately to discuss how to proceed.  Do not attempt to adjust your dose yourself.
* **Q: Is bevacizumab a cure for cancer?** A: No, bevacizumab is a treatment to help manage cancer and slow or stop its progression, not a cure.  It's typically used in combination with other therapies.
