# Fluorescein

**Generic Name: Fluorescein**

**Usage:**

Fluorescein is primarily used as a diagnostic dye.  Its most common application is in ophthalmology, where it's used to stain the cornea and other structures of the eye to highlight abnormalities. This allows doctors to visualize corneal abrasions, ulcers, foreign bodies, and other eye injuries or conditions that might otherwise be difficult to detect.  It's also helpful in assessing the integrity of the tear film and identifying leaks in the drainage system of the eye.  Beyond ophthalmology, fluorescein can be used in other diagnostic procedures, such as angiography (to visualize blood vessels) and in detecting leaks in the gastrointestinal tract.  These latter uses are less common than its ophthalmic applications.

**Dosage:**

Dosage varies greatly depending on the specific application and the form of fluorescein used.  There's no standard dosage for adults or children that can be universally applied.

* **Ophthalmic Use:**  Typically, a few drops of a fluorescein sodium solution (usually 0.25% to 2%) are instilled directly into the eye.  The specific concentration and amount will be determined by the ophthalmologist.
* **Angiography:**  The dosage and administration method for angiography using fluorescein are significantly different and are determined by the administering physician.  It's usually administered intravenously and requires careful monitoring.
* **Gastrointestinal Use:** The use of fluorescein in this context is specialized and requires precise guidance from a medical professional.


**Side Effects:**

* **Common:** Temporary stinging or burning sensation in the eye (with ophthalmic use).  Slight discoloration of urine (yellow-green) which is generally harmless and temporary.
* **Less Common but Serious:**  Allergic reactions (rash, itching, swelling, difficulty breathing) are possible, though rare.  In rare cases, intravenous administration can cause nausea, vomiting, or diarrhea.
* **Note:** If any adverse effects occur, it's crucial to seek immediate medical attention.

**How it Works:**

Fluorescein is a fluorescent dye. This means it absorbs light at one wavelength and emits light at a longer wavelength. When illuminated with a blue light source (like a Wood's lamp), the fluorescein glows bright yellow-green. This fluorescence highlights areas where it has accumulated, allowing doctors to visualize structures or abnormalities otherwise invisible to the naked eye. In the case of eye exams, this highlights damaged or compromised areas of the cornea. In angiography, it outlines the path of blood flow in the vessels.

**Precautions:**

* **Allergy:**  Individuals with known allergies to fluorescein should inform their doctor.
* **Pregnancy/Breastfeeding:**  While generally considered safe, pregnant or breastfeeding women should consult their doctor before using fluorescein.
* **Other Medications:**  There are generally no significant drug interactions, but it is advisable to inform your doctor of all medications you are taking.
* **Contact Lenses:** Contact lenses should be removed before applying ophthalmic fluorescein.

**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does the yellow discoloration of urine last?**  A: Usually a few hours, but can last up to 24 hours.
* **Q: Is fluorescein safe for children?** A:  Generally safe when used appropriately under the supervision of a healthcare professional.
* **Q: How is fluorescein stored?** A:  Store according to the instructions on the product label, usually at room temperature away from light and moisture.
* **Q:  Can I drive after using fluorescein eye drops?**  A:  It is generally advisable to wait until your vision clears before driving.
