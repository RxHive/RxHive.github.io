# Insulin Isophane

**Generic Name: Insulin Isophane (NPH Insulin)**

**Usage:**

Insulin Isophane, often called NPH insulin, is a type of intermediate-acting insulin used primarily to manage **type 1 diabetes** and **type 2 diabetes**.  Its primary use is to control high blood sugar (hyperglycemia) in people who don't produce enough insulin or whose bodies don't use insulin effectively.  While its main application is in managing long-term blood sugar control, it may also be used in conjunction with other types of insulin (such as rapid-acting insulin) to achieve better blood glucose regulation throughout the day.  The doctor will determine the best approach based on the individual's needs.


**Dosage:**

Dosage of Insulin Isophane is highly individualized and depends on several factors, including the patient's age, weight, overall health, blood glucose levels, diet, and physical activity. It is crucial to remember that **this information is not a substitute for a doctor's prescription.** A healthcare professional will determine the appropriate starting dose and adjust it as needed based on regular blood glucose monitoring.

* **Form:** Insulin Isophane is administered via subcutaneous injection (under the skin), usually in the abdomen, thigh, or upper arm. It is *not* available as a tablet or syrup.
* **Adults:**  Starting doses typically range from 0.5 to 1 unit per kilogram of body weight daily, but this can vary significantly.
* **Children:** Dosage for children is also individualized based on the child's weight, age, and blood sugar levels.  A pediatrician or endocrinologist will make these determinations.
* **Administration:**  Always follow your doctor's instructions precisely on how to administer the injection.  Proper injection technique is crucial to prevent complications.


**Side Effects:**

* **Common:** Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar), injection site reactions (redness, swelling, itching), weight gain.
* **Less Common but Serious:** Severe hypoglycemia (leading to loss of consciousness), allergic reactions (rash, hives, difficulty breathing), lipohypertrophy (thickening of the fat tissue at the injection site).
* **Important Note:** If you experience any adverse effects, especially severe ones, contact your healthcare provider immediately.


**How it Works:**

Insulin Isophane is a modified form of human insulin.  Unlike rapid-acting insulin, it's designed to release its glucose-lowering effects gradually over a longer period (typically 12-18 hours).  It works by binding to receptors on cells throughout the body, allowing glucose (sugar) to enter the cells and be used for energy. This helps to lower blood sugar levels and prevent the complications associated with uncontrolled diabetes.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:**  Insulin Isophane is contraindicated in individuals with severe hypoglycemia (low blood sugar) or those who are allergic to insulin.
* **Drug Interactions:**  Many medications can interact with insulin, affecting its effectiveness or increasing the risk of hypoglycemia.  Inform your doctor of all medications, supplements, and herbal remedies you are taking.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  Insulin Isophane can be used during pregnancy and breastfeeding, but close monitoring of blood glucose levels is necessary.  Discuss this with your doctor.
* **Elderly:** Older adults may be more susceptible to hypoglycemia and require careful dose adjustments.
* **Children:**  Children require careful monitoring and individualized dosage adjustments.
* **Alcohol Consumption:**  Alcohol can increase the risk of hypoglycemia.  Limit alcohol intake while using insulin.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does Insulin Isophane last?** A: Its effect typically lasts for 12-18 hours.
* **Q: How should I store Insulin Isophane?** A: Store unopened vials in the refrigerator. Once opened, store it at room temperature, protected from light and heat.  Do not freeze.  Discard after the expiration date.
* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Contact your doctor or healthcare provider immediately. Do not try to "catch up" by taking a double dose.
* **Q: Can I stop taking Insulin Isophane suddenly?** A: No, never stop taking Insulin Isophane abruptly without consulting your doctor.  Sudden cessation can lead to dangerous complications.
* **Q: What are the symptoms of low blood sugar?** A: Symptoms include sweating, trembling, weakness, dizziness, confusion, and rapid heartbeat.
