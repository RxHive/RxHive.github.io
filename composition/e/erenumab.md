# Erenumab

**Generic Name: Erenumab**

**Usage:**

Erenumab is a prescription medication primarily used to prevent migraine headaches in adults.  It's specifically designed for those experiencing chronic or episodic migraine with a significant impact on their lives.  "Chronic migraine" refers to experiencing migraine headaches on 15 or more days per month, while "episodic migraine" involves fewer days but still causes significant disability. While its primary use focuses on migraine prevention, some studies are exploring its potential in treating other headache disorders, though these are not yet standard uses.  It's crucial to remember that erenumab is a preventative medication and will *not* stop a migraine once it has started.

**Dosage:**

Erenumab is administered via subcutaneous injection (under the skin).  It is currently not available in oral or other forms.  The standard dosage for adults is typically 70 mg or 140 mg administered once monthly, depending on individual response and the doctor's assessment. Dosage adjustments are based on individual tolerance and effectiveness and aren't usually determined by age, weight, or severity except as noted by the prescribing physician.  There are currently no approved dosages for children.  Always follow the instructions provided by your doctor or pharmacist precisely.

**Side Effects:**

Common side effects can include:

* Constipation
* Fatigue
* Injection site reactions (pain, swelling, redness)
* Nausea
* Dizziness

Less common, but potentially serious side effects warrant immediate medical attention and include:

* Allergic reactions (rash, hives, difficulty breathing)
* Serious injection site reactions


If you experience any adverse effects, especially those listed as serious, contact your healthcare provider immediately.

**How it Works:**

Erenumab works by targeting a protein called calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP). CGRP is a molecule involved in the transmission of pain signals in the brain and is thought to play a significant role in the development of migraine headaches. By binding to and blocking the effects of CGRP, erenumab prevents these pain signals from reaching the brain, thus reducing the frequency and severity of migraine attacks.

**Precautions:**

* **Allergic reactions:** Individuals with known allergies to erenumab or any of its components should avoid its use.
* **Pregnancy and breastfeeding:**  The safety of erenumab during pregnancy and breastfeeding hasn't been fully established.  Consult your healthcare provider to discuss the risks and benefits if you are pregnant, breastfeeding, or planning to become pregnant.
* **Drug interactions:** While significant drug interactions are rare, inform your doctor about all medications, supplements, and herbal remedies you are taking to assess potential interactions.
* **Liver or kidney impairment:** Patients with severe liver or kidney problems may require dose adjustments; this should be determined by their physician.
* **Older adults:**  While specific dosage adjustments aren't generally needed, older adults might be more sensitive to side effects.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does it take to work?** A:  The effectiveness of erenumab can vary. It typically takes several weeks to see a noticeable reduction in migraine frequency,  so consistent use is crucial.
* **Q: Can I stop taking it suddenly?** A: No.  Always consult your doctor before stopping erenumab.  Sudden discontinuation might lead to a return of migraines.
* **Q: How should I store it?** A: Store erenumab in the refrigerator, as directed on the packaging.
* **Q: Is it addictive?** A: No, erenumab is not considered an addictive medication.
* **Q: What if I miss a dose?** A: Contact your doctor immediately to discuss how to proceed.  Do not take a double dose to make up for a missed dose.
