# Enclomiphene

**Generic Name: Enclomiphene**

**Usage:**

Enclomiphene is primarily used to treat female infertility caused by anovulation (failure to release an egg). It's a selective estrogen receptor modulator (SERM), meaning it acts similarly to estrogen in some tissues but blocks its effects in others.  This specific action helps to stimulate ovulation in women who don't ovulate regularly or at all. While its primary use focuses on infertility treatment, research is exploring its potential in other areas, but these remain investigational and not yet established for widespread clinical use.

**Dosage:**

Dosage is determined by a healthcare professional and will vary based on individual response and condition.  Typically, enclomiphene is administered orally in tablet form.  A common starting dose for adults is 25mg daily for a specific number of days, usually within a specific cycle phase as directed by a doctor.  The dose might be adjusted, and the treatment duration might also be altered based on the patient’s response (ovulation status, etc.) and individual needs. There is no established pediatric use of Enclomiphene.  Always follow the prescribed dosage and administration instructions provided by your doctor; never alter the dose on your own.

**Side Effects:**

Common side effects can include:

* Hot flashes
* Headaches
* Nausea
* Abdominal pain or bloating
* Mood changes (including irritability or depression)
* Visual disturbances (blurred vision)
* Vaginal bleeding
* Ovarian enlargement (in some cases)


Less common but potentially serious side effects include multiple pregnancies (twins or triplets) and ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome (OHSS), which can be life-threatening.  Any new or worsening symptoms should be reported to your doctor immediately.


**How it Works:**

Enclomiphene works by selectively binding to estrogen receptors in the hypothalamus and pituitary gland. In the hypothalamus, it antagonizes (blocks) estrogen's action, which leads to increased secretion of gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH). This, in turn, stimulates the pituitary gland to release follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) and luteinizing hormone (LH).  The increased FSH and LH levels promote follicular growth in the ovaries, ultimately leading to ovulation.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Enclomiphene is contraindicated in patients with liver disease, severe kidney disease, uncontrolled thyroid problems, uterine fibroids, or unexplained vaginal bleeding.  It should also be avoided in women with a history of ovarian hyperstimulation syndrome or multiple pregnancies.
* **Drug Interactions:**  Some medications may interact with enclomiphene, potentially altering its effectiveness or increasing the risk of side effects.  Always inform your doctor about all medications you are taking, including over-the-counter drugs and herbal supplements.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  Enclomiphene is not recommended during pregnancy or breastfeeding.  Effective contraception should be used while taking this medication.
* **Older Adults:**  Dosage adjustments might be necessary for older adults due to potential age-related changes in organ function.
* **Children:**  Enclomiphene is not typically used in children.
