# Edaravone

**Generic Name: Edaravone**

**Usage:**

Edaravone is primarily used to treat amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), also known as Lou Gehrig's disease.  ALS is a progressive neurodegenerative disease that affects nerve cells in the brain and spinal cord, leading to muscle weakness and atrophy. Edaravone is not a cure for ALS, but it's designed to slow the progression of the disease and potentially improve the function of some patients.  While its primary use is in ALS, research is ongoing to explore its potential applications in other neurological conditions involving oxidative stress, though these are not currently standard clinical uses.

**Dosage:**

Edaravone is administered intravenously (IV), meaning it's injected directly into a vein.  The standard dosage for adults with ALS is typically 30 mg given once daily for 14 consecutive days, followed by a 14-day break, then repeated.  This cycle of treatment continues as directed by a physician.  There is no standard pediatric dosage as the use in children is not established. Dosage adjustments are not typically based on weight or specific disease severity but are determined by a physician based on overall patient health and response to therapy. There are currently no oral or other forms of Edaravone available.  The injection must be administered by a healthcare professional.

**Side Effects:**

Common side effects can include:

*   Nausea
*   Vomiting
*   Diarrhea
*   Bruising or bleeding at the injection site
*   Rash
*   Headache
*   Dizziness
*   Fatigue

Less common but serious side effects may occur, such as allergic reactions (including anaphylaxis).  Any unusual or worsening symptoms should be reported immediately to a doctor.

**How it Works:**

Edaravone is a free radical scavenger, meaning it helps neutralize harmful molecules called free radicals.  In ALS and other neurodegenerative diseases, an overabundance of free radicals contributes to nerve cell damage. By reducing the amount of free radicals, Edaravone may help protect nerve cells and slow the progression of the disease. Its exact mechanism of action in ALS remains an area of ongoing research, but its free radical scavenging properties are central to its therapeutic effect.
