# Equine Rabies Immunoglobulin

**Generic Name:** Equine Rabies Immunoglobulin (ERIG)

**Usage:**

Equine Rabies Immunoglobulin is a life-saving medication used primarily to prevent rabies after exposure to a potentially rabid animal.  This is known as post-exposure prophylaxis (PEP).  It's crucial to understand that ERIG is *not* a treatment for *existing* rabies; it's preventative.  Rabies, once it progresses to clinical symptoms, is almost always fatal.  ERIG works by providing immediate, passive immunity, supplying the body with ready-made antibodies against the rabies virus. This gives the body a significant head-start in fighting off the virus before it can establish a serious infection.  While its primary use is post-exposure prophylaxis, in very rare circumstances, it might be used in conjunction with rabies vaccine in cases of suspected rabies exposure where the vaccine is unavailable or delayed.


**Dosage:**

ERIG is administered intramuscularly (into the muscle) by a healthcare professional.  Dosage is determined by the severity of the exposure (e.g., bite vs. scratch, location of the bite, animal's rabies status) and the patient's weight.  There isn't a standard dosage for adults and children in a way that other drugs might have; instead, the amount administered is calculated based on the specific circumstances of each case, usually determined by a physician experienced in managing rabies exposure.  Detailed guidelines are available from relevant public health authorities and infectious disease specialists.   

**Side Effects:**

* Local reactions at the injection site: Pain, swelling, redness, and itching are common.
* Systemic reactions (affecting the whole body): Fever, chills, headache, muscle aches, nausea, and rash are possible but less frequent.
* Serum sickness: A rare but serious allergic reaction that can manifest as fever, rash, joint pain, and swollen lymph nodes. This is a serious risk because ERIG is derived from horse serum.

**It's extremely important to report any side effects to your doctor immediately.**  Severe allergic reactions require prompt medical attention.


**How it Works:**

ERIG contains rabies-specific antibodies derived from the blood of horses that have been immunized against rabies.  When injected, these antibodies directly neutralize the rabies virus, preventing it from infecting nerve cells and spreading throughout the body.  This provides immediate, albeit temporary, protection while the body's immune system mounts its own response to the virus through subsequent rabies vaccination.  The antibodies in ERIG act like a temporary shield, buying precious time for the body's own defense mechanisms.

**Popular Brand Names (India):**

Specific brand names for ERIG can vary by location and availability within India.  It is recommended to consult a physician or pharmacist for current brand availability.  Often the product is simply identified as "Equine Rabies Immunoglobulin"  rather than by a specific proprietary brand name.


**Precautions:**

* **Allergy to horse serum:**  ERIG is derived from horses, making it contraindicated (should not be used) in individuals with a known hypersensitivity to horse serum or equine proteins.  A thorough allergy history is essential before administration.
* **Drug interactions:**  While rare, potential interactions with other medications should be discussed with a healthcare provider.
* **Pregnancy and breastfeeding:** The benefits of ERIG in preventing rabies outweigh the potential risks during pregnancy and breastfeeding in most cases; however, this should be carefully evaluated on a case-by-case basis by a physician.
* **Children and older adults:**  ERIG is used in children and older adults when indicated for rabies prophylaxis, but dose adjustment might be considered based on age and weight.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does ERIG protection last?** A: ERIG provides temporary protection—typically for a few weeks—while the body develops its own immunity through vaccination.
* **Q: Is ERIG safe?** A: ERIG is generally safe when administered correctly, but allergic reactions are possible.  It's crucial to report any adverse effects to your doctor immediately.
* **Q: How is ERIG stored?** A: ERIG requires refrigeration.  Always follow the storage instructions provided by the manufacturer.
* **Q: What should I do if I'm bitten by an animal I suspect might have rabies?** A: Seek immediate medical attention.  The sooner you receive PEP, the better your chances of preventing the disease.
