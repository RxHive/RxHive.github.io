# Human insulin

**Generic Name:** Human Insulin

**Usage:**

Human insulin is a crucial medication primarily used to treat **diabetes mellitus**, specifically type 1 diabetes and certain types of type 2 diabetes.  In type 1 diabetes, the body doesn't produce insulin, a hormone essential for regulating blood sugar levels.  Human insulin replaces this missing hormone, allowing glucose (sugar) from the bloodstream to enter cells for energy.  In some cases of type 2 diabetes, when the body's own insulin production is insufficient or becomes resistant to its effects, human insulin supplements the body's efforts to maintain healthy blood sugar control.

While its primary use is in managing diabetes, human insulin may also have secondary uses in situations requiring rapid blood sugar correction, such as during diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA), a serious complication of diabetes. It might also be used in certain other medical conditions under strict medical supervision, though this is not its typical application.

**Dosage:**

Dosage of human insulin varies significantly depending on the individual's needs, determined through regular blood glucose monitoring and close collaboration with a healthcare professional.  It's not possible to provide a standardized dosage here. The form, dosage, and timing are personalized and often adjusted based on factors like:

* **Type of diabetes:** Type 1 typically requires multiple daily injections, while type 2 might involve insulin only when blood sugar control is inadequate with other treatments.
* **Age:**  Dosage adjustments are often made for children and older adults.
* **Weight:**  Body weight influences insulin requirements.
* **Blood glucose levels:**  Regular monitoring guides dose adjustments.
* **Physical activity:** Exercise can impact insulin sensitivity.
* **Diet:**  Food intake directly influences insulin needs.


Human insulin is administered via injection, primarily through syringes or insulin pens.  There are various types of insulin, including rapid-acting, short-acting, intermediate-acting, and long-acting, each with its own onset, peak, and duration of action. The specific type and dosage are determined by the doctor based on the patient's individual needs and preferences.  Oral administration of insulin isn't possible because it would be digested in the stomach before it could reach the bloodstream.

**Side Effects:**

* **Common:** Hypoglycemia (low blood sugar), injection site reactions (redness, swelling, itching), weight gain.
* **Less Common but Serious:** Severe hypoglycemia, lipohypertrophy (fatty deposits at injection sites), hypersensitivity reactions (allergic reactions).

**Note:**  Any new or worsening symptoms should be reported to a doctor immediately.


**How it Works:**

Human insulin mimics the action of naturally produced insulin. It binds to insulin receptors on cells, signaling them to take up glucose from the bloodstream.  This lowers blood sugar levels, preventing the complications associated with uncontrolled hyperglycemia (high blood sugar).  Different types of insulin have slightly different profiles regarding how quickly they start working and how long their effects last, allowing for tailored management of blood sugar levels throughout the day.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:**  Human insulin is generally contraindicated for individuals with known hypersensitivity to insulin or any of its components.
* **Drug Interactions:**  Many medications can interact with insulin, potentially affecting blood sugar levels.  It’s crucial to inform your doctor about all medications, including over-the-counter drugs and supplements.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  Insulin use during pregnancy and breastfeeding requires close monitoring by a healthcare professional to ensure both maternal and fetal/infant well-being.
* **Elderly:**  Older adults may require dose adjustments due to changes in metabolism and potential kidney or liver impairment.
* **Children:**  Dosage for children is carefully calculated based on weight and other factors.

**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does human insulin last?** A: This depends on the type of insulin used.  Each type has a specific onset, peak, and duration.  Your doctor will explain the specifics of your prescribed insulin.
* **Q: How should I store human insulin?** A:  Store unopened insulin vials in the refrigerator.  Opened vials and insulin pens should be refrigerated or stored at room temperature, but avoid extreme temperatures or direct sunlight.  Always check the manufacturer's instructions.
* **Q: What should I do if I experience low blood sugar?** A: Treat low blood sugar immediately with a fast-acting carbohydrate source like glucose tablets or juice.  Consult your doctor or follow their instructions for managing hypoglycemia.
* **Q: Can I mix different types of insulin in the same syringe?** A:  Possibly, but it's crucial to follow your doctor's instructions carefully.  Mixing is not always recommended or possible.
* **Q:  Are there long-term side effects?** A: Long-term complications associated with diabetes itself (e.g., heart disease, kidney disease, nerve damage) can be reduced with good blood sugar control using insulin. However, long-term side effects of insulin are less common and usually related to injection site reactions or the need for careful monitoring and management.
