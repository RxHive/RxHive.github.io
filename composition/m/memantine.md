# Memantine

**Generic Name: Memantine**

**Usage:**

Memantine is primarily used to treat moderate to severe Alzheimer's disease.  Its main role is to slow the progression of cognitive decline associated with this neurodegenerative condition.  This means it can help improve memory, thinking, and reasoning abilities in individuals suffering from Alzheimer's, although it doesn't cure the disease itself.  While less common, memantine may also be used off-label in some cases to manage symptoms of other types of dementia, but this is always under strict medical supervision.  It's crucial to understand that memantine's effectiveness varies from person to person, and it's not suitable for all individuals with dementia.

**Dosage:**

Dosage of memantine is typically tailored to the individual's needs and tolerance.  A common starting dose for adults is a low initial dose, gradually increased over several weeks to reach the therapeutic level. This gradual increase helps minimize potential side effects.  The standard maintenance dose is usually in the range of 10-20 mg daily, taken as a single dose or divided into two doses.  Children generally do not receive memantine for Alzheimer's disease; its use in children is extremely rare and only considered under very specific circumstances and with close medical monitoring.  Memantine is usually available in oral tablet form; liquid formulations may be available in some regions.  The medication should be taken as directed by a physician; never adjust the dose yourself.

**Side Effects:**

Common side effects of memantine include:

* Dizziness
* Headache
* Constipation
* Nausea
* Vomiting
* Confusion
* Sleep disturbances
* Fatigue

Less common but serious side effects can include:

* Allergic reactions (rash, hives, difficulty breathing)
* Hallucinations
* High blood pressure
* Liver problems

If you experience any adverse effects, especially those listed as serious, it's vital to contact your healthcare provider immediately.

**How it Works:**

Memantine works by modulating the activity of a neurotransmitter called glutamate.  In Alzheimer's disease, glutamate levels can become excessively high, potentially damaging brain cells.  Memantine acts as a non-competitive NMDA receptor antagonist, meaning it selectively blocks the excessive activity of glutamate at NMDA receptors in the brain. This helps to protect brain cells from damage and potentially improve cognitive function.  It's a complex process, but the core action is to regulate glutamate activity and help mitigate the detrimental effects of its overactivity in Alzheimer's disease.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Memantine should not be used in individuals with known hypersensitivity to the drug.
* **Drug Interactions:** Memantine can interact with other medications, particularly those affecting kidney function or the central nervous system.  It is crucial to inform your doctor about all medications, supplements, and herbal remedies you are taking.
* **Pregnant and Breastfeeding Women:**  The use of memantine during pregnancy and breastfeeding requires careful consideration and should only be undertaken under strict medical supervision due to a lack of extensive safety data.
* **Older Adults:** Older adults may be more susceptible to side effects and require careful dose adjustments.
* **Kidney Disease:**  Memantine is primarily excreted through the kidneys; therefore, individuals with kidney impairment may require dose adjustments or close monitoring.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: Can I stop taking memantine suddenly?**  A: No.  Stopping memantine abruptly can lead to withdrawal symptoms.  Always follow your doctor's instructions for tapering off the medication.
* **Q: How should I store memantine?** A: Store memantine in a cool, dry place, away from direct sunlight and moisture, as directed on the packaging.
* **Q: Is memantine addictive?** A:  Memantine is not considered an addictive medication.
* **Q: Will memantine cure my Alzheimer's?** A: No, memantine does not cure Alzheimer's disease. It helps manage symptoms and may slow the progression of cognitive decline.
* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Take the missed dose as soon as you remember, unless it's almost time for your next dose.  Do not double the dose.
