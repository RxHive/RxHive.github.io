# Metoclopramide

**Generic Name: Metoclopramide**

**Usage:**

Metoclopramide is primarily used to relieve symptoms related to nausea and vomiting.  Its main application is in managing gastroparesis, a condition where the stomach empties food too slowly.  This can lead to discomfort, bloating, and persistent nausea. Metoclopramide helps speed up the emptying of the stomach, alleviating these symptoms.  It's also used to prevent nausea and vomiting caused by chemotherapy or radiotherapy, and in certain cases, it might be prescribed to treat symptoms of gastroesophageal reflux disease (GERD), though other medications are typically preferred for this condition.  Occasionally, it's used to help with the passage of a barium swallow during diagnostic imaging.  The specific use will always depend on a doctor's assessment of the individual's needs.

**Dosage:**

Dosage varies greatly depending on the individual's age, weight, condition, and the specific reason for prescribing metoclopramide.  A doctor will always determine the appropriate dose.  Generally, for adults, oral dosages range from 5mg to 10mg three or four times daily.  Injectable forms are also available, with dosages determined by a healthcare professional.  Children's dosages are significantly lower and always determined and administered by a doctor or other qualified healthcare provider based on weight and the condition being treated.  Never administer metoclopramide to a child without a doctor's explicit prescription and guidance.  The medication comes in various forms including tablets, syrups, and injections.  Always follow the instructions on the prescription label and any specific directions given by your healthcare provider.


**Side Effects:**

* **Common:** Drowsiness, fatigue, diarrhea, restlessness, anxiety, and extrapyramidal symptoms (involuntary muscle movements like tremors or spasms, particularly in higher doses).
* **Less Common but Serious:**  Tardive dyskinesia (a serious, potentially irreversible movement disorder characterized by involuntary movements of the face, mouth, and tongue), neuroleptic malignant syndrome (a rare but life-threatening condition involving high fever, muscle rigidity, and altered mental status), and seizures.  Allergic reactions are also possible.


**How it Works:**

Metoclopramide works by affecting the brain's signals to the digestive system.  It primarily blocks the action of dopamine, a neurotransmitter that, in certain situations, can slow down the movement of food through the gastrointestinal tract. By blocking dopamine's effects, metoclopramide enhances the motility of the stomach and upper intestines, promoting quicker emptying and reducing nausea and vomiting.  It also increases the pressure in the lower esophageal sphincter, which can help prevent stomach acid reflux.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Metoclopramide should not be used by individuals with pheochromocytoma (a tumor of the adrenal gland), certain types of gastrointestinal obstruction, or known hypersensitivity to the medication.
* **Drug Interactions:** Metoclopramide can interact with various medications, including certain antidepressants, anticoagulants, and opioids. It is crucial to inform your doctor about all medications you are currently taking.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:** The use of metoclopramide during pregnancy and breastfeeding should be carefully considered and only undertaken under the strict supervision of a doctor, as potential risks to the fetus or infant may exist.
* **Elderly and Children:**  Elderly patients and children are generally more susceptible to side effects. Dosage adjustments may be necessary.  Again, a physician will guide the dosing.
* **Driving and Operating Machinery:**  Because drowsiness is a common side effect, avoid driving or operating machinery until you know how this medicine affects you.

**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does it take for metoclopramide to work?** A: The onset of action varies but usually happens relatively quickly, within minutes to an hour for relieving nausea and vomiting. However, the full therapeutic effect for conditions like gastroparesis may take longer to develop.

* **Q: Can I stop taking metoclopramide suddenly?** A:  No. Always follow your doctor's instructions regarding when and how to stop taking this medication.  Sudden cessation may lead to withdrawal symptoms.

* **Q: How should I store metoclopramide?** A: Store the medication as directed on the label, usually in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and heat. Keep it out of reach of children.

* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A:  Take the missed dose as soon as you remember, unless it's almost time for your next dose.  Never double up on doses.

* **Q:  Are there any long-term risks associated with metoclopramide?** A: Long-term use carries a slightly increased risk of developing tardive dyskinesia. Your doctor will weigh the benefits of continued treatment against this risk.
