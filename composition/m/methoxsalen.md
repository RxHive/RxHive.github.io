# Methoxsalen

**Generic Name: Methoxsalen**

**Usage:**

Methoxsalen is a medication primarily used in the treatment of psoriasis and vitiligo.  It belongs to a class of drugs called psoralens, which are photosensitizing agents.  This means they make the skin more sensitive to ultraviolet (UV) light.  The primary use involves combining methoxsalen with exposure to UVA light (PUVA therapy).  This combination therapy helps to slow the rapid skin cell growth in psoriasis and stimulates repigmentation in vitiligo.  While less common now, methoxsalen has also historically been used to treat certain types of lymphoma, though other treatments are generally preferred today.  It's crucial to understand that methoxsalen is *not* used alone; its effectiveness relies on its interaction with UV light.

**Dosage:**

Dosage of methoxsalen varies greatly depending on the specific condition being treated, the individual's age and weight, and the type of UV light used.  A doctor will determine the precise dosage and treatment plan.  It's typically administered orally, usually as a tablet, before exposure to UVA light.  There is no standardized pediatric dosage as it is rarely used for children. Precise instructions regarding dosage, frequency, and duration of treatment will be given by a healthcare professional and should be followed meticulously.  The dosage will be adjusted based on the response to treatment and any side effects experienced.

**Side Effects:**

* **Common:** Nausea, vomiting, headache, sunburn-like reactions (erythema), itching, skin discoloration.
* **Less Common but Serious:**  Severe sunburn, blistering, skin inflammation (erythema multiforme), cataracts, premature aging of the skin, increased risk of skin cancer (with long-term PUVA therapy).  
* **Important Note:**  Any unusual skin reactions, severe sunburn, or persistent side effects should be reported to a doctor immediately.

**How it Works:**

Methoxsalen works by absorbing into the skin and interacting with DNA. Upon exposure to UVA light, it forms a compound that affects cell growth and skin pigmentation.  In psoriasis, this helps to slow down the rapid cell turnover characteristic of the disease. In vitiligo, it promotes the regrowth of pigment-producing cells (melanocytes).  The exact mechanisms by which it achieves these effects are still under investigation.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Methoxsalen should not be used in individuals with known allergies to psoralens, severe liver or kidney disease, lupus, or porphyria.
* **Drug Interactions:**  Methoxsalen can interact with several medications.  It is essential to inform your doctor of all medications, supplements, and herbal remedies being taken.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  Methoxsalen is generally avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding due to potential risks to the developing fetus or infant.
* **Sunlight Exposure:**  Avoid excessive sun exposure, both before and after treatment. Use sunscreen with a high SPF.
* **Children and Older Adults:** Methoxsalen is generally not recommended for children or older adults unless specifically prescribed and closely monitored by a doctor due to increased risk of side effects.

**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does treatment last?** A: The duration of treatment varies widely depending on the condition and individual response, ranging from several weeks to months.
* **Q: How is the medication stored?** A:  Store methoxsalen tablets in a cool, dry place, away from direct sunlight and moisture.  Follow the storage instructions provided on the label.
* **Q: Is methoxsalen addictive?** A: Methoxsalen is not considered an addictive medication.
* **Q:  What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: If you miss a dose, consult your doctor or pharmacist for guidance.  Do not double the dose to make up for a missed one.
