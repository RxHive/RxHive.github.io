# Vigabatrin

**Generic Name: Vigabatrin**

**Usage:**

Vigabatrin is primarily used to treat epilepsy, specifically infantile spasms (West syndrome) and complex partial seizures in adults and children.  Infantile spasms are a severe form of epilepsy that usually begins in infancy.  Complex partial seizures involve impaired awareness and often unusual movements or sensations.  While less common, vigabatrin may also be considered for other types of epilepsy when other medications haven't been effective, although this is usually done under specialist supervision.  It's important to understand that vigabatrin is a second-line treatment, meaning it's generally considered after other anti-epileptic drugs have been tried.


**Dosage:**

Dosage varies significantly depending on the patient's age, weight, and the specific type and severity of their epilepsy.  A healthcare professional will determine the appropriate dosage.  

* **Adults:**  Typical starting doses range from 1000mg to 1500mg daily, usually divided into two or three doses.  The dose may be gradually increased based on response and tolerability, up to a maximum of 3000mg daily.

* **Children:** Dosing for children is also individualized and carefully monitored by a physician.  It's often based on weight and starts at a lower dose, gradually increasing as needed.

Vigabatrin is typically administered orally as tablets or capsules.  Precise instructions on how to take the medication will be provided by a doctor or pharmacist.   


**Side Effects:**

Common side effects can include:

* Dizziness
* Fatigue
* Tremor
* Drowsiness
* Weight gain
* Nausea
* Vomiting
* Headache

Less common, but more serious side effects include:

* Vision problems (including visual field constriction or loss) – this can be permanent, making regular eye exams crucial.
* Psychiatric effects such as depression, anxiety, or behavioral changes
*  Neurological problems (rare)

If you experience any concerning side effects, particularly visual changes or significant psychological changes, contact your healthcare provider immediately.


**How it Works:**

Vigabatrin works by inhibiting an enzyme called GABA-transaminase. This enzyme breaks down gamma-aminobutyric acid (GABA), a neurotransmitter that plays a crucial role in reducing neuronal excitability in the brain. By blocking the breakdown of GABA, vigabatrin increases its levels, thereby helping to reduce the frequency and severity of seizures.


**Precautions:**

* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  Vigabatrin should generally be avoided during pregnancy unless the benefits clearly outweigh the risks. Its potential effects on a developing fetus are still being investigated.  It's also important to discuss with a healthcare professional if you are breastfeeding.
* **Visual Field Defects:**  The most serious side effect is permanent visual field loss.  Regular eye exams are crucial during treatment.
* **Drug Interactions:** Vigabatrin can interact with other medications, so it is vital to inform your doctor of all medications, including over-the-counter drugs and herbal supplements, you are taking.
* **Liver and Kidney Function:**  Patients with liver or kidney problems may require dosage adjustments.  Regular monitoring of these functions is usually necessary.
* **Older Adults and Children:**  Dosage adjustments and close monitoring are crucial for both older adults and children due to increased risk of side effects.

**FAQs:**

* **Q: Can I stop taking vigabatrin suddenly?**  A: No, stopping vigabatrin suddenly can increase the risk of seizures.  Your doctor will gradually reduce your dose to minimize this risk.

* **Q: How should I store vigabatrin?** A: Store vigabatrin in a cool, dry place, away from direct sunlight and moisture, as directed on the label.

* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Take the missed dose as soon as you remember, unless it's nearly time for the next dose.  Never take a double dose to make up for a missed one.

* **Q:  Is vigabatrin addictive?** A: Vigabatrin is not considered an addictive drug in the same way as opioids or stimulants. However, abrupt discontinuation can lead to withdrawal symptoms, highlighting the importance of a gradual tapering schedule under medical supervision.

* **Q:  How often do I need to see my doctor while taking vigabatrin?** A:  Your doctor will advise on the frequency of checkups based on your individual needs and response to treatment. This usually involves regular monitoring of your seizure activity and potential side effects, especially vision.
