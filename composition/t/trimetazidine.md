# Trimetazidine

**Generic Name: Trimetazidine**

**Usage:**

Trimetazidine is primarily used to treat angina pectoris, a type of chest pain caused by reduced blood flow to the heart muscle.  It's particularly helpful for patients experiencing angina that isn't adequately controlled by other medications or who experience angina despite lifestyle modifications.  While its primary use is in managing angina, some studies suggest potential benefits in other conditions affecting the heart and brain, such as certain types of vertigo and possibly improving neurological function after a stroke.  However, these secondary uses are still under investigation and aren't universally accepted as standard treatment.  It's crucial to only use trimetazidine as prescribed by a doctor.

**Dosage:**

Dosage varies depending on the individual and the specific condition being treated.  A doctor will determine the appropriate dosage based on factors like age, overall health, and response to treatment.  Typically, for adults treating angina, the starting dose is 35 mg twice daily, which may be increased to 70 mg twice daily in some cases.  There is no established pediatric dosage; trimetazidine is generally not recommended for children.  Trimetazidine is usually available in tablet form.  It should be taken with food to minimize potential gastrointestinal upset. The medication should be swallowed whole and not crushed or chewed.

**Side Effects:**

Common side effects of trimetazidine are generally mild and include:

* Nausea
* Vomiting
* Diarrhea
* Abdominal pain
* Dizziness
* Headache
* Fatigue

Less common, but more serious side effects may include:

*  Changes in heart rhythm (arrhythmias)
*  Low blood pressure (hypotension)
*  Parkinsonism-like symptoms (tremor, rigidity, slow movement)

If you experience any side effects, particularly those listed as more serious, it's important to contact your doctor immediately.


**How it Works:**

Trimetazidine works by inhibiting the enzyme long-chain 3-ketoacyl-CoA thiolase.  This enzyme is involved in fatty acid oxidation, which is the process the heart uses to generate energy.  By inhibiting this enzyme, trimetazidine shifts the heart's energy source from fatty acids to glucose.  Since glucose metabolism is less affected by reduced blood flow, this helps the heart maintain its energy production even during periods of reduced oxygen supply, thereby alleviating angina symptoms.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Trimetazidine is generally not recommended for patients with known hypersensitivity (allergy) to the drug.
* **Drug Interactions:** Trimetazidine may interact with other medications, especially those affecting the heart or nervous system.  It's essential to inform your doctor of all medications, including over-the-counter drugs and herbal supplements, you are taking.
* **Specific Populations:**  Caution should be exercised when using trimetazidine in pregnant or breastfeeding women, as its safety in these populations is not fully established.  As mentioned earlier, it's generally not recommended for children.  Older adults may be more susceptible to side effects and require close monitoring.  Patients with kidney or liver problems may also require dosage adjustments.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: Can I stop taking trimetazidine suddenly?** A: No, you should never stop taking trimetazidine abruptly without consulting your doctor.  Stopping suddenly could lead to a worsening of angina symptoms.  Your doctor will advise you on the appropriate way to gradually reduce your dosage if necessary.

* **Q: How should I store trimetazidine?** A: Store trimetazidine tablets in a cool, dry place, away from direct sunlight and moisture, as directed on the medication packaging.

* **Q: Is trimetazidine addictive?** A: No, trimetazidine is not considered an addictive medication.

* **Q: Can I drink alcohol while taking trimetazidine?** A:  Moderate alcohol consumption may be acceptable, but it's best to discuss this with your doctor. Excessive alcohol use can negatively impact heart health and interact with certain medications.

* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Take the missed dose as soon as you remember, unless it's almost time for your next dose.  Do not take a double dose to make up for a missed one.
