# Terfenadine

**Generic Name: Terfenadine**

**Usage:**

Terfenadine is an antihistamine that was primarily used to relieve the symptoms of allergic rhinitis (hay fever) and urticaria (hives).  Its primary action is to block the effects of histamine, a substance released by the body during an allergic reaction.  This blockage leads to a reduction in symptoms such as sneezing, runny nose, itchy eyes, and hives.  While it was effective for these conditions, terfenadine is no longer widely available in many countries due to concerns about its potential for serious heart rhythm problems (see Precautions).  It's crucial to understand that this information is for educational purposes only and should not be interpreted as an endorsement of its use.  If you require treatment for allergies, consult a healthcare professional for current, safe and effective options.


**Dosage:**

*Because terfenadine is no longer widely available, dosage information is provided for historical context only and should not be used to self-medicate.*  Prescriptions, if ever obtained, would have included specific instructions tailored to the individual patient.  Generally, adult dosages were in tablet form and varied depending on the severity of symptoms. Pediatric dosages, if available, would have been adjusted based on weight and age.  There were no injectable forms of terfenadine.  Any information found outside of a valid prescription should be disregarded.


**Side Effects:**

* **Common:**  Drowsiness, dry mouth, headache, dizziness.
* **Less Common but Serious:**  Prolonged QT interval (a heart rhythm abnormality that can lead to potentially fatal heart arrhythmias),  palpitations.

**Important Note:**  If you experience any side effects, particularly heart palpitations or irregular heartbeat, seek immediate medical attention.


**How it Works:**

Terfenadine works by competitively blocking histamine H1 receptors. Histamine, released during allergic reactions, binds to these receptors, triggering the symptoms of allergy. By preventing histamine from binding, terfenadine reduces the inflammatory response and alleviates symptoms.  It's important to note that terfenadine itself is not metabolized into an active substance that affects the body; however, its metabolite, fexofenadine, does show some antihistaminic activity.  The risk associated with terfenadine's effect on the heart was linked to its metabolic pathway and the potential for a build-up of its metabolites.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:**  Terfenadine should not be used in patients with known hypersensitivity to the drug or its components.
* **Drug Interactions:**  Terfenadine interacted significantly with certain medications, particularly those affecting the liver's ability to metabolize drugs (CYP3A4 inhibitors such as ketoconazole and erythromycin).  These interactions increased the risk of serious heart rhythm problems.
* **Specific Warnings:**  Terfenadine carried a significant risk of QT prolongation, a condition leading to potentially fatal cardiac arrhythmias. This risk was particularly high in patients with pre-existing heart conditions, liver dysfunction, or those taking certain other medications. Pregnant and breastfeeding women should avoid terfenadine.  Its use in children was also limited due to safety concerns.  This medication should never be taken without the express instructions of a medical professional.
