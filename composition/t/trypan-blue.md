# Trypan Blue

**Generic Name: Trypan Blue**

**Usage:**

Trypan blue is a dye, not a typical medication in the sense of treating a disease directly. Its primary use is as a diagnostic tool, particularly in research and laboratory settings.  It's not prescribed for treating conditions in humans in the way a typical pharmaceutical drug would be. Its main application is in:

* **Assessing cell viability:** Trypan blue is used to distinguish between live and dead cells.  Live cells possess intact cell membranes that exclude the dye, while dead cells with compromised membranes take up the dye, staining them blue. This makes it invaluable in cell culture work and other biological research.  This assessment is vital for many experiments studying cell growth, death, and response to treatments.

* **Identifying certain types of cancers:**  In some instances, trypan blue might be used in conjunction with other diagnostic methods to help visualize cancerous tissue during surgery. This is a less common application, and its role is often supplementary to other, more definitive diagnostic procedures.

It's crucial to understand that trypan blue is *not* a treatment for any specific human disease. Its role is purely diagnostic or research-based.

**How it Works:**

Trypan blue is an anionic dye. It works by staining cells only when their cell membrane is compromised, indicating cell death.  Live cells with intact membranes actively exclude the dye, maintaining their normal appearance.  The dye’s negative charge prevents it from entering healthy cells with intact membranes. This principle is the basis for its use in assessing cell viability.  In the rare instances of its use in visualizing tissue, the dye acts as a contrast agent to enhance visibility.
