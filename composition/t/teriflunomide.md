# Teriflunomide

**Generic Name: Teriflunomide**

**Usage:**

Teriflunomide is primarily used to treat relapsing-remitting multiple sclerosis (RRMS).  RRMS is a disease where the immune system attacks the protective covering of nerves in the brain and spinal cord, leading to a range of symptoms like muscle weakness, numbness, vision problems, and fatigue.  Relapsing-remitting refers to periods of attacks (relapses) followed by periods of remission where symptoms improve or disappear.  Teriflunomide helps to reduce the frequency and severity of these relapses. While not currently approved for other conditions, research is ongoing to explore its potential in other autoimmune diseases.


**Dosage:**

Teriflunomide is typically available as an oral tablet.  The recommended starting dose for adults with RRMS is generally 14 mg once daily for three days.  Following this loading dose, the maintenance dose is typically 7 mg daily.  Dosage adjustments are rarely necessary but may be considered based on individual patient response and tolerance, always under the guidance of a physician.  Teriflunomide is not approved for use in children.  There's no syrup or injectable form currently available.  It is crucial to follow your doctor's prescribed dosage and not alter it without their consultation.


**Side Effects:**

* **Common Side Effects:** Diarrhea, nausea, vomiting, hair thinning or loss, elevated liver enzymes (detected through blood tests), increased risk of infections.
* **Less Common but Serious Side Effects:**  Severe allergic reactions (anaphylaxis), serious liver damage, severe lung problems, low white blood cell count (neutropenia).  These are rare but require immediate medical attention.

**If you experience any adverse effects, contact your healthcare provider immediately.**


**How it Works:**

Teriflunomide works by interfering with the body's immune response.  Specifically, it inhibits an enzyme called dihydroorotate dehydrogenase (DHODH), which is crucial for the production of pyrimidines.  Pyrimidines are essential building blocks of DNA and RNA, which are necessary for the rapid proliferation of immune cells. By slowing down this process, teriflunomide reduces the activity of immune cells that contribute to the damage seen in RRMS, thus helping to lessen the frequency and severity of relapses.


**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Teriflunomide should not be used by individuals with severe liver disease, severe kidney disease, or those who are pregnant or breastfeeding.  Women of childbearing potential must use effective contraception during treatment and for a period after treatment discontinuation.
* **Drug Interactions:** Teriflunomide may interact with certain medications.  Inform your doctor about all medications, including over-the-counter drugs, herbal supplements, and vitamins, you are taking.
* **Vulnerable Groups:**  Special caution is warranted in older adults due to increased risk of side effects.  The drug is contraindicated in pregnancy and breastfeeding.  Children should not use this medication.
* **Safety Measures:** Regular blood tests to monitor liver function and blood cell counts are necessary during treatment.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does it take to see results?** A:  The effects of teriflunomide are not immediate.  It may take several weeks or months to observe a reduction in relapse frequency.

* **Q: Can I stop taking teriflunomide suddenly?** A: No.  Stopping teriflunomide abruptly can lead to a rapid increase in immune activity.  Your doctor will guide you on a safe discontinuation process which may involve taking a specific medication to hasten the elimination of teriflunomide from your system.

* **Q: How should I store teriflunomide?** A: Store at room temperature, away from moisture and heat.  Keep out of reach of children.

* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Take the missed dose as soon as you remember, unless it is almost time for your next dose.  Do not double the dose.  Consult your doctor or pharmacist if you have concerns about missed doses.

* **Q: Is teriflunomide addictive?** A: No, teriflunomide is not considered addictive.
