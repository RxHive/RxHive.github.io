# Prochlorperazine

**Generic Name: Prochlorperazine**

**Usage:**

Prochlorperazine is an antiemetic and antipsychotic medication.  Its primary use is to control nausea and vomiting, particularly in cases of severe nausea and vomiting caused by various conditions.  This includes post-operative nausea and vomiting, nausea and vomiting associated with cancer chemotherapy, and nausea from other causes.  It's also sometimes used to treat severe nausea and vomiting related to migraine headaches or vertigo. 

As an antipsychotic, prochlorperazine is used to treat certain psychotic disorders, specifically those characterized by symptoms such as hallucinations and delusions. However,  it's less commonly used for this purpose now compared to newer antipsychotic medications due to the potential for more significant side effects. It's sometimes used to help manage severe anxiety and agitation in specific situations. The decision to use prochlorperazine for psychotic disorders is usually made when other treatments have been ineffective or unsuitable.


**Dosage:**

Dosage varies considerably depending on the condition being treated, the patient's age and weight, and the specific formulation of the medication (tablets, suppositories, injection).  It's crucial to follow a doctor's prescription precisely.  

* **Adults (Nausea and Vomiting):** Dosage ranges typically start low and are adjusted based on response.  The usual range is 5-10 mg every 6-8 hours as needed.
* **Adults (Psychotic Disorders):**  Doses for this indication are higher and more precisely determined by a healthcare professional.
* **Children:**  Prochlorperazine is generally not recommended for children under 2 years old. Dosages for older children are significantly lower than adult doses and carefully determined by the physician based on weight and response.
* **Forms:** Prochlorperazine is available in tablets, suppositories (rectal), and injectable forms.  The route of administration will be determined by the healthcare provider based on the patient’s condition and ability to take oral medication.

**Never exceed the prescribed dosage.** Always consult a doctor or pharmacist before making changes to your dosage.


**Side Effects:**

* **Common Side Effects:**  Drowsiness, dizziness, dry mouth, blurred vision, constipation, low blood pressure (hypotension).
* **Less Common but Serious Side Effects:**  Extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS) like muscle stiffness, tremors, involuntary muscle movements (tardive dyskinesia – a potentially irreversible condition),  neuroleptic malignant syndrome (NMS – a life-threatening condition), seizures, jaundice, allergic reactions.

**If you experience any side effects, particularly those listed as serious, consult your healthcare provider immediately.**


**How it Works:**

Prochlorperazine works by blocking dopamine and serotonin receptors in the brain.  By affecting these neurotransmitters, it reduces the signals that trigger nausea and vomiting.  Its antipsychotic effects are also related to its ability to block dopamine receptors, thus reducing some of the symptoms of psychosis.


**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:**  Prochlorperazine should not be used by people with known allergies to the medication, certain types of blood disorders, or those with a severe blockage in the intestines.
* **Drug Interactions:** Prochlorperazine can interact with many other drugs, including other medications that affect the central nervous system, antihistamines, and alcohol.  Always inform your healthcare provider of all medications you are taking, including over-the-counter drugs and herbal remedies.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  The use of prochlorperazine during pregnancy and breastfeeding requires careful consideration by a healthcare professional. The potential benefits must be weighed against the potential risks to the mother and the child.
* **Elderly and Children:**  Older adults and children are more susceptible to the side effects of prochlorperazine.  Dosage adjustments and careful monitoring are necessary.
* **Driving and Operating Machinery:**  Prochlorperazine can cause drowsiness and dizziness; therefore, avoid driving or operating machinery until you know how the medication affects you.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does it take for prochlorperazine to work?**  A: The onset of action varies depending on the administration route and the condition being treated.  For nausea, relief may be felt within minutes to hours after an injection, while oral forms take longer.
* **Q: Can I drink alcohol while taking prochlorperazine?** A: No, alcohol can enhance the sedative effects of prochlorperazine and should be avoided.
* **Q: How should I store prochlorperazine?** A: Store prochlorperazine at room temperature, away from moisture and heat.
* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Take the missed dose as soon as you remember, unless it is almost time for the next dose. Do not double the dose.
* **Q:  Is prochlorperazine addictive?** A:  Physical dependence is not typically associated with prochlorperazine, but tolerance can develop, requiring adjustments in dosage.
