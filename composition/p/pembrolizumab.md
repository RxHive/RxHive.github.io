# Pembrolizumab

**Generic Name:** Pembrolizumab

**Usage:**

Pembrolizumab is a type of immunotherapy medication known as a checkpoint inhibitor.  Its primary use is in treating various types of cancer.  It works by targeting and blocking a protein called PD-1, which is found on certain immune cells.  By blocking PD-1, pembrolizumab helps the body's immune system recognize and attack cancer cells more effectively.

Pembrolizumab is approved for use in several cancers, including but not limited to:

* **Melanoma:**  Both advanced melanoma (that has spread) and melanoma that can be surgically removed but has a high risk of recurrence.
* **Lung Cancer:**  Specifically, non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), often used after other treatments haven't been successful.
* **Head and Neck Cancer:** Advanced forms that haven't responded well to other treatments.
* **Urothelial Cancer:**  A type of bladder cancer, often used after other treatments.
* **Classical Hodgkin Lymphoma:**  A type of lymphoma, usually after other treatment options have been exhausted.
* **Microsatellite Instability-High (MSI-H) or Mismatch Repair Deficient (dMMR) Cancers:** This applies to various cancers with this specific genetic characteristic.

The specific use of pembrolizumab and the success rate vary depending on the type and stage of cancer, as well as the patient's overall health.  It's important to remember that this list is not exhaustive, and your doctor will determine if pembrolizumab is the appropriate treatment for you.


**Dosage:**

Pembrolizumab is administered intravenously (IV) by a healthcare professional.  Dosage is determined by the doctor based on several factors, including the type and stage of cancer, body weight, and overall health.  There isn't a standardized "one-size-fits-all" dosage.  The typical dose is usually given every two or three weeks, but this schedule can vary considerably.  There is no pediatric formulation; dosage in children is determined on a case-by-case basis under strict medical supervision.  It's crucial to follow your doctor's prescribed dosage and schedule precisely.


**Side Effects:**

Pembrolizumab, like other immunotherapies, can cause side effects, ranging from mild to severe.  Some common side effects include:

* Fatigue
* Diarrhea
* Nausea
* Rash
* Cough
* Constipation
* Loss of appetite
* Itchiness

More serious, but less common, side effects can include:

* Severe allergic reactions
* Pneumonitis (inflammation of the lungs)
* Colitis (inflammation of the colon)
* Hepatitis (inflammation of the liver)
* Nephritis (inflammation of the kidneys)
* Hypothyroidism (underactive thyroid)
* Endocrinopathies (hormone imbalances)

**It's vital to contact your doctor immediately if you experience any concerning side effects.**  Early intervention is crucial in managing potential complications.


**How it Works:**

Pembrolizumab works by targeting the PD-1 protein on immune cells.  Cancer cells often use PD-1 to evade detection and destruction by the immune system. Pembrolizumab binds to PD-1, blocking its interaction with another protein called PD-L1, which is found on some cancer cells. This blockade removes the "brakes" on the immune system, allowing it to recognize and attack cancer cells more effectively.


**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Pembrolizumab is contraindicated in individuals with known hypersensitivity to the drug or its components.
* **Drug Interactions:**  Pembrolizumab can interact with other medications.  It is essential to inform your doctor of all medications you are taking, including over-the-counter drugs and supplements.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:**  Pembrolizumab should generally be avoided during pregnancy and breastfeeding.  Discuss the risks and benefits with your doctor if pregnancy is planned or occurs during treatment.
* **Older Adults and Children:**  Special caution is warranted in these populations, and dosage adjustments might be needed. Close monitoring is usually necessary.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long will I need to take pembrolizumab?** A: The duration of treatment depends on your response to the medication and the type of cancer you have. Your doctor will determine this based on regular monitoring.

* **Q: How is pembrolizumab stored?** A:  Storage instructions will be provided by your pharmacist.  Generally, it involves refrigeration.

* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Contact your doctor immediately to reschedule your dose. Do not attempt to adjust the schedule yourself.

* **Q:  Is pembrolizumab a cure for cancer?** A: Pembrolizumab is a treatment, not a cure. Its goal is to control cancer growth and improve survival rates.

* **Q: Are there long-term effects from taking pembrolizumab?** A: Some individuals may experience long-term effects, even after treatment is stopped.  Regular check-ups are essential to monitor for any lasting issues.  Your doctor can provide more information about potential long-term effects.
