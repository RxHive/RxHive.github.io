# Psoralen

**Generic Name: Psoralen**

**Usage:**

Psoralen is a photosensitizing drug, meaning it makes the skin more sensitive to ultraviolet (UV) light.  Its primary use is in the treatment of psoriasis and vitiligo.  In **psoriasis**, psoralen is used in conjunction with ultraviolet A (UVA) light therapy, a treatment known as PUVA (psoralen plus UVA) therapy. This combination helps to slow the rapid skin cell growth characteristic of psoriasis, reducing inflammation and scaling.  In **vitililigo**, PUVA therapy aims to stimulate repigmentation in affected skin areas by increasing melanin production.  While less common, psoralen may also be used in the treatment of other skin conditions like mycosis fungoides (a type of cutaneous T-cell lymphoma) and atopic dermatitis, again usually in combination with UVA light therapy.  It's crucial to understand that psoralen itself doesn't cure these conditions; it's a component of a larger treatment strategy.

**Dosage:**

Psoralen dosage is highly individualized and determined by a physician based on the specific condition being treated, the patient's age, weight, and overall health.  It's typically administered orally in tablet form, although topical applications are possible in some cases. The dosage of psoralen is followed by a carefully controlled exposure to UVA light.  Dosage for adults typically ranges from 0.6 to 2.4 mg per kilogram of body weight, but this is just a general guideline, and the actual prescribed dose will vary greatly depending on the patient and the treatment plan. Pediatric dosages are significantly lower and must be precisely calculated by a healthcare professional.  There are no standardized syrup or injection forms commonly used for psoralen treatment.  Detailed instructions for administration and timing relative to UV light exposure will be provided by the prescribing physician.

**Side Effects:**

Common side effects of psoralen can include:

* Nausea
* Vomiting
* Headache
* Dizziness
* Skin redness and burning (especially after UVA exposure)
* Itching
* Skin dryness
* Photosensitivity (increased sun sensitivity)

Less common, but more serious side effects may include:

* Severe sunburn
* Skin blistering
* Premature aging of the skin
* Cataracts
* Increased risk of skin cancer (long-term risk with repeated PUVA treatments)


If you experience any adverse effects, particularly severe reactions, contact your doctor immediately.

**How it Works:**

Psoralen works by interacting with DNA in skin cells. When psoralen is ingested and the skin is subsequently exposed to UVA light, the psoralen molecules absorb the UVA radiation. This triggers a reaction that causes the DNA to become cross-linked, which slows down cell division.  In psoriasis, this helps to reduce the rapid skin cell turnover. In vitiligo, it may stimulate melanin production by influencing melanocyte (pigment-producing cell) activity.  The exact mechanisms by which psoralen improves vitiligo are still not fully understood.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Psoralen is contraindicated in individuals with porphyria (a group of inherited disorders affecting heme synthesis), severe liver or kidney disease, and those with a history of severe sun sensitivity or photosensitivity reactions.  Pregnancy and breastfeeding are also generally contraindications.
* **Drug Interactions:** Psoralen can interact with various medications, including certain antibiotics and diuretics.  Always inform your doctor about all medications you are taking.
* **Warnings:**  Psoralen can significantly increase the risk of sunburn. Strict adherence to the physician's instructions regarding UVA exposure is crucial. Regular eye protection (special UV-blocking glasses) is essential during and after PUVA therapy to prevent cataracts.  Individuals with a history of skin cancer should exercise extreme caution and discuss the risks with their physician before undergoing PUVA treatment.
* **Vulnerable Groups:**  The use of psoralen in pregnant or breastfeeding women, children, and older adults requires careful consideration and monitoring due to increased risk of side effects.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does psoralen treatment last?** A: The duration of treatment varies greatly depending on the condition and individual response. It can range from several weeks to months.

* **Q: Can I use tanning beds instead of UVA therapy?** A:  No, tanning beds should not be used. The intensity and type of UVA radiation used in PUVA therapy are carefully controlled and regulated by a medical professional. Using tanning beds is dangerous and could lead to severe complications.

* **Q: How should I store psoralen tablets?** A: Store psoralen tablets as directed on the label, usually in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight and moisture.

* **Q:  What should I do if I miss a dose?** A:  Do not attempt to double up on doses.  Contact your doctor or pharmacist for guidance.

* **Q: Is psoralen addictive?** A: No, psoralen is not considered an addictive substance.
