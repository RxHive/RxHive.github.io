# Apomorphine

**Generic Name: Apomorphine**

**Usage:**

Apomorphine is primarily used to treat Parkinson's disease.  Specifically, it's employed to manage "off" periods – those frustrating times when Parkinson's medication's effects wear off, leading to a return of symptoms like rigidity, tremors, and difficulty moving.  It's a short-acting medication, meaning its effects are noticeable fairly quickly but don't last for extended periods.  It's typically reserved for patients whose symptoms aren't adequately controlled by other Parkinson's medications. While not its primary use, apomorphine may also be used to treat severe nausea and vomiting, particularly in cases of drug-induced emesis.  However, its use in this context is less common than its application in Parkinson's disease management.  Its use in these contexts requires careful medical supervision.

**Dosage:**

Apomorphine is administered through different routes, primarily subcutaneous injection (under the skin) and, less frequently, orally as a sublingual tablet (dissolved under the tongue). Dosage is highly individualized and determined by a physician based on the patient's response and tolerance.  There are no standardized dosages for children as its use in this population is very limited.  For adults with Parkinson's disease, the initial dose is typically low and gradually increased as needed under close medical supervision to find the optimal balance between symptom relief and side effects.  The medication comes in varying strengths, requiring precise measurement and administration. Specific instructions on how to administer the injection will be provided by a healthcare professional.  Oral forms, if available, will have specific instructions regarding their administration.


**Side Effects:**

Common side effects include:

* Nausea and vomiting
* Dizziness and lightheadedness
* Low blood pressure (hypotension)
* Drowsiness
* Sleepiness
* Yawning
* Flushing
* Injection site reactions (if injected)

Less common but serious side effects include:

* Severe hypotension leading to fainting
* Irregular heartbeat (arrhythmias)
* Breathing problems
* Allergic reactions
* Severe nausea and vomiting


**Any adverse effects should be reported immediately to a healthcare provider.**


**How it Works:**

Apomorphine is a dopamine agonist, meaning it mimics the effects of dopamine, a neurotransmitter crucial for movement control.  In Parkinson's disease, dopamine-producing brain cells are damaged, leading to movement problems.  Apomorphine binds to dopamine receptors in the brain, stimulating them and helping to alleviate Parkinson's symptoms.  The mechanism behind its antiemetic (anti-nausea and vomiting) effects is less clearly understood but may involve interactions with dopamine receptors in the brain's vomiting center.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Apomorphine is contraindicated in patients with a known hypersensitivity to the drug or sulfites.  It should also be avoided in patients with certain types of heart conditions, severe respiratory conditions, or those with a history of severe sleep attacks (narcolepsy).
* **Drug Interactions:** Apomorphine can interact with several medications, including MAO inhibitors, neuroleptics, and other dopamine agonists.  It's crucial to inform your doctor about all medications, supplements, and herbal remedies you are taking.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:** Apomorphine's use during pregnancy and breastfeeding requires careful evaluation of risks and benefits by a healthcare professional. It's generally not recommended unless absolutely necessary.
* **Elderly and Children:**  Apomorphine should be used with extra caution in elderly individuals due to increased sensitivity to its side effects. As previously mentioned, its use in children is exceptionally rare and requires special consideration.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does apomorphine take to work?**  A:  The onset of action is usually rapid, within minutes of subcutaneous injection.  The duration of action is relatively short, typically lasting a few hours.

* **Q: How should I store apomorphine?** A: Store according to the instructions provided with your specific medication. Generally, it should be kept in a cool, dry place away from direct sunlight.

* **Q: Is apomorphine addictive?** A:  Like other dopamine agonists, apomorphine can be habit-forming, particularly with prolonged use.  Close monitoring by a healthcare provider is important.

* **Q: What should I do if I miss a dose?** A: Never double a dose.  Consult your healthcare provider for guidance on missed doses.
