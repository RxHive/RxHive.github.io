# Adenosine

**Generic Name:** Adenosine

**Usage:**

Adenosine is a medication primarily used to treat paroxysmal supraventricular tachycardia (PSVT).  PSVT is a type of rapid heart rhythm originating above the ventricles (the lower chambers of the heart).  Adenosine works quickly to interrupt this abnormal rhythm, allowing the heart to return to a normal sinus rhythm.  It's administered intravenously (IV) and its effects are usually very short-lived.  While its primary use is in treating PSVT, adenosine may also be used in diagnostic procedures, specifically to help assess the electrical pathways of the heart. This helps cardiologists pinpoint the origin of abnormal heart rhythms.


**Dosage:**

Adenosine is administered intravenously (IV) only, typically by a healthcare professional in a hospital or clinic setting. The dosage is usually determined by the patient's weight and the specific condition being treated.  The usual adult dose for PSVT is a rapid bolus injection of 6 mg, followed by a second dose of 12 mg if the first is ineffective.   A third dose of 12 mg may be considered if needed.  Children's doses are calculated based on their weight, typically starting at 0.1 mg/kg. Precise dosage and administration must always be guided by a physician.  There are no oral or other forms of adenosine currently available.


**Side Effects:**

* **Common:**  The most common side effect is a temporary feeling of flushing (redness) of the skin, shortness of breath, chest pressure, and palpitations.  These usually subside quickly.
* **Less Common, but Serious:**  More serious, though less frequent, side effects include:  bradycardia (slow heart rate), hypotension (low blood pressure), heart block, and in rare instances, allergic reactions.
* **Note:** Any side effects, even if mild, should be reported to a healthcare provider.  Seek immediate medical attention if you experience severe or worsening symptoms.


**How it Works:**

Adenosine works by slowing down the conduction of electrical impulses in the heart.  Specifically, it affects the AV node (atrioventricular node), which is the electrical gateway between the upper and lower chambers of the heart.  By temporarily slowing or blocking the impulses at this node, adenosine disrupts the rapid, irregular heartbeat characteristic of PSVT, allowing the heart's normal rhythm to resume.  Its effects are very short-lived, typically lasting only a few seconds.


**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:** Adenosine should not be used in individuals with severe heart block, sick sinus syndrome (a disorder affecting the heart's natural pacemaker), or certain types of asthma.  
* **Drug Interactions:** Adenosine's effects can be altered by other medications, particularly those affecting the heart.  Full disclosure of all medications to your physician is crucial before adenosine administration.
* **Vulnerable Groups:**  Caution is advised when using adenosine in pregnant women, children, and older adults.  The risks and benefits must be carefully weighed by a physician before administration in these groups.
* **Allergies:** Patients with known allergies to adenosine or similar medications should inform their doctor.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How long does adenosine stay in my system?** A:  Adenosine's effects are very short-lived, usually lasting only seconds to minutes.  It's rapidly metabolized by the body.
* **Q: Can I take adenosine at home?** A: No, adenosine is administered intravenously and requires the supervision of a healthcare professional in a medical setting.
* **Q: What should I do if I experience side effects?** A:  Report any side effects to your doctor or nurse immediately.  Seek immediate medical attention if you experience severe symptoms.
* **Q: How is adenosine stored?** A: Adenosine should be stored according to the manufacturer's instructions, typically at a controlled room temperature.
