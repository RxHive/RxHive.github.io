# Albumin

**Generic Name: Albumin**

**Usage:**

Albumin is a protein naturally found in human blood.  Medically, it's used primarily to treat conditions involving low blood albumin levels (hypoalbuminemia), which can stem from various causes like severe liver disease, kidney disease, burns, trauma, or malnutrition.  These conditions can lead to fluid imbalances, causing swelling (edema) and potentially life-threatening complications.  Albumin, administered intravenously (IV), helps restore the blood's volume and maintain fluid balance within the body's tissues.  While its primary use is in addressing hypoalbuminemia, albumin may also be used as a volume expander in situations of significant blood loss during surgery or trauma, acting as a temporary substitute until the body can naturally replenish its own albumin.  It is also sometimes used during procedures like plasmapheresis.

**Dosage:**

Dosage of albumin is highly individualized and determined by a healthcare professional based on the patient's specific condition, age, weight, and the severity of hypoalbuminemia or fluid loss. It's administered intravenously, and the dosage is typically expressed as a volume (e.g., 50 mL, 100 mL, or more) or as a total protein goal.  Children require adjusted doses based on their weight and the specific medical need, calculated by a physician.  There are no oral or other forms of albumin available; it must be administered intravenously by a trained medical professional.  The rate of administration is also carefully controlled to avoid complications.

**Side Effects:**

* **Common:**  Fever, chills, nausea, vomiting, headache, and flushing.  These are usually mild and transient.
* **Less Common but Serious:**  Allergic reactions (ranging from mild rash to severe anaphylaxis), heart problems (arrhythmias), pulmonary edema (fluid in the lungs), and clotting abnormalities.  In rare cases, transmission of infectious agents can occur if the albumin isn't properly screened.

**Important:**  Any adverse effects, even if seemingly minor, should be reported immediately to a healthcare provider.

**How it Works:**

Albumin is a major protein in blood plasma.  It plays a crucial role in maintaining the osmotic pressure of the blood, meaning it helps to regulate the balance of fluid between the blood vessels and the tissues. When albumin levels are low, fluid leaks out of the blood vessels into the tissues, leading to swelling (edema). Administering albumin intravenously increases the blood's osmotic pressure, drawing fluid back into the bloodstream and thus reducing edema and improving circulation. This helps restore the body’s fluid balance and blood volume.

**Precautions:**

* **Contraindications:**  Albumin should not be used in patients with severe heart failure, severe anemia, or known allergy to albumin.
* **Drug Interactions:**  While specific interactions are uncommon, it’s crucial to inform the healthcare provider of all medications (prescription and over-the-counter) being taken to assess any potential issues.
* **Pregnancy and Breastfeeding:** Albumin use during pregnancy and breastfeeding should be carefully evaluated by a physician to assess the risk-benefit ratio for both mother and child.  The benefits usually outweigh the risks in cases of severe hypoalbuminemia.
* **Older Adults and Children:** Doses need to be adjusted based on age and overall health status.  Close monitoring is necessary in these groups.


**FAQs:**

* **Q: How is albumin stored?** A: Albumin solutions should be stored according to the manufacturer's instructions, typically at room temperature, protected from light.  Do not freeze.

* **Q: How long does it take to work?** A: The effects of albumin administration are generally seen relatively quickly, helping to restore fluid balance within hours.  However, the underlying cause of hypoalbuminemia needs to be addressed for long-term improvement.

* **Q: Is albumin addictive?** A: No, albumin is not addictive.

* **Q: Can I buy albumin over the counter?** A: No, albumin is a prescription medication administered intravenously by healthcare professionals and is not available over the counter.
